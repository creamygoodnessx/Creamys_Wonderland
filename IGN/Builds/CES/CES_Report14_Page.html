> Yesterday, I asked JD if I could borrow his soldering iron for a while. He gave the iron and some solder to my parents who were visiting JD to see my nephews. I was too tired to do any soldering that day, but I did manage to get to it today. My goal was to strengthen the power button by replacing its flimsy wire with something stronger. About a month ago, I found an audio/video cable that appeared thicker than the wire I planned to replace. I began the task of strengthening the power button by cutting the AV cable in half with a pair of scissors, and then cutting the AV connectors off of one of the halves. On that same half of cable, I stripped the sheaths off of one of the ends, and then cut away the shielding. This exposed two thinner sheathed wires, one red and one grey. I then stripped off the sheathing of the two wires, exposing the wire, which was only slightly thicker than the cable I was replacing. I repeated the process on the other end of the cable, so that the cable had a total of four exposed wires; two on each end.

> My next task was to prepare the two brass screws, which were the contact points for the power button, for tinning. I separated the wire from the power switch by removing the black electrical tape from the screws, and I was surprised at how sticky and dirty they were. So I cleaned them off with a dry paper towel and a tiny bit of soap. I then cut the jumper connector off of the cable just below the heat shrink, and then carefully stripped off the red and white sheaths. Now, I was ready to tin all of the surfaces for soldering. I removed the two brass screws from the power switch, because I was afraid the tinning process would melt the thin plastic medicine bottle which was the body of the switch. The soldering process was a bit tricky to get used to, but soon I got the hang of it. The solder kept clumping up on the screws, so I turned the wattage up on the iron from 15 watts to 30. Once I did that, the solder melted like butter. I made sure to not get any solder on the front of the bolts, because if I did, I wouldn't be able to screw the bolts back into the switch's holes. Once the bolts were tinned, I put them back into place.

> Using the setting of 15 watts on the soldering iron, I tinned the four exposed wires of the cable, and then the two exposed wires of the jumper connector. I then twisted the white wire of the jumper with the grey wire on one end of the cable, and then the red wire on the same end of the cable to the red wire of the jumper block. I decided to solder the cable to the switch next, since this was the more difficult end of the cable to solder, and I wanted to get it over with first. I peeled the two wires of the cable apart a little further, and then wrapped the exposed wires around the brass screws. Not too much of the wires were exposed, so they didn't wrap around the screws as much or as well as I would have liked them to. I couldn't get the tinned surfaces to solder together, so I was forced to use additional solder. I completed the soldering, but I accidentally loosened the screws from their mounts. In order to get them to fit more solidly, I used the soldering iron to melt a gluestick, and glued around the outside of the switch where the bolts were. The result was a tighter fit, but not as tight as they were before. I soldered the other end of the cable to the jumper connector to finish the strengthening of the switch. Finally, I wrapped electrical tape around the exposed wires of the jumper connector in order to prevent any accidental short circuiting.

 

10/9/2004 - It's almost six in the morning, and I'm going over to my brother Peter's later on today to use his drill press. I spent a few hours this morning going over how I'm going to assemble CES's chassis. To cut a long story short, I found that the holes drilled in two of my four 7" angles were in the wrong place. If I were to drill holes in my 4" beams to accommodate the holes already in the 7" beams, the 4" angle holes would be way off to one side, and the bolts would look awkward against the painted background. When I painted my 4" beams, I made sure the patterns would go with the bolts, so that the position of the bolts went with the images. On some of the 4" beams are used "?" blocks or 3D blocks on the top or bottom of the beam. The bolts are supposed to go in the center of these blocks, making them look like they're part of the image. Because the bolt holes in the two 7" beams are out of place, they are now useless to me as parts of CES. This means I'll have to order two replacement 7" beams from OnlineMetals.com. When I checked the site, I found that I could get two more of such beams for a total of $2.80, which I can barely afford since my project's budget is bottoming out. If the shipping and handling costs too much, I'll have to cut back on components that I have yet to order, and this could mean getting a less powerful Celeron processor.

> It looks pretty obvious now that I'll have to rethink how I'm going to assemble my chassis. Instead of putting together the bottom assembly first, and then the side assemblies, and then bolting the three parts together, I have decided to base all of the chassis' construction on the four inch angle irons. This is because the exact positions of the bolts on the 4" beams matter more than on any other beams. In order to see if this was a good way of assembling the chassis, I simulated the construction bit by bit by holding the beams together with Scotch tape. I found from the simulation that some of the beams have a slight curve to them and are not true right angles.

> Here's how CES's chassis will now be put together. I will start by drilling the holes in my 4" angle irons, since I know exactly where those holes need to go; in the middle of the top and bottom square inch blocks of each beam. I will fit a 7" beam into a 4" beam, pushing the 7" beam as far into the curved inner edge of the 4" beam as I can while avoiding the curve and keeping the two beams firmly pressed against each other. I would then use the hole in the 4" beam to mark the drill hole in the 7" beam, drill through the mark and then bolt the two beams together. Then, I will take the corresponding 4" beam, and attach it to the other side of the 7" beam in the same manner. I would then repeat with the bottom 7" beam and two 4" beams on the other side of CES. When the left and right sides of CES are bolted together, I will put the 12" beams on top of the 7" beams, one in front and one in back, and they will bridge the two sides of CES together. I will make sure to bring the edges of the 12" beams to the curved inner edges of the 7" beams, but just enough to keep the 12" beams resting firmly within the 7" beams. I will then mark the drill holes on either side of the 12" beams, drill the holes, use those holes to mark the drill holes in the 7" beams, and again drill accordingly. Once the drill holes of the skeleton are in place, I will shave a bit of the 12" long edge off of the bottom panel to make it fit in the bottom of the skeleton, mark the drill holes on the panel, drill the holes, and then bolt the panel into place. Finally, I will put the side panels in their place between the 4" and 7" beams, mark and drill the holes, and then bolt the side panels into place. It's just after 7PM and I've got to pack all this stuff up so I can get some sleep, however little I manage to get.
 

9/27/2004 - I have less than $18 in my budget, and last night I went to OnlineMetals.com and determined that replacing my rounded-cornered 7" and 12" angle irons with flat-cornered versions would cost me over twenty bucks, meaning I'd most likely have to downgrade one of my components to afford it. So naturally, that's something I want to avoid. Just before midnight, I took another look at my angle irons and side panels, hoping that I could at least salvage something. While studying the chassis parts for nearly two hours, I realized that the chassis was not in as bad a condition as JD and I first believed. There were three basic problems that prevented me from building my chassis with my current plans. First, there will be two bolt holes on either side of each angle iron, and the holes are oriented so that the two bolts will be at right angles of each other. The two bolts appear to run right into each other, and at first, I thought this was going to be a problem. However, the bolts only run into each other when they're going through the angle iron alone. When the chassis is completed, most of the bolts will in fact go through an angle iron, a panel, and then another angle iron. This will greatly reduce how much the end of each bolt is exposed, and leave just enough room for a nut to be fastened onto it. The bolts will not run into each other, so the first problem becomes a non-issue.

> The second problem is a possible wobbling of the chassis parts after the bolts are in place. The bolts may not hold tightly enough to keep the chassis from wobbling. But I know from my experience in building the top hatch that bolts can hold two parts just fine, but I forgot to tell JD that. I tested a pair of nuts and bolts on two of the 7" beams which had the bolt holes drilled into them, but couldn't get a real solid fit; the beams kept wobbling. But if I had some kind of tool to use to tighten the bolts instead of using my hands, I think the bolts would have held much tighter.

> The third problem is that the angle irons have rounded corners, and this may interfere with fitting them together. However, I've noticed that the 12" beam, which is riveted to the 7" beam, does not go all the way into the 7" beam, but stops at the 7" beam's rounded corner. If all the angle irons are riveted together in this fashion, the rounded corners won't matter. This will mean that I won't have to order the flat-cornered beams, and won't have to downgrade any of my components.

> I'm going to address these issues the next time I talk to JD. If he agrees that the angle irons are still salvageable as is, I plan to try building the chassis again this coming weekend. After popping out the rivet in the 12" and 7" beam, I plan on drilling the rest of the holes in the chassis, and then bolting everything together. The bottom panel is too wide to fit into the bottom assembly as it is, so we might have to saw off some of the edge. But I'm not getting my hopes up just yet; there's still plenty of ways I can screw up the chassis.

> I decided it was time to apply the nail polish to the angle irons, and I began with the Fire Flower beam, since I considered that to be the most expendable of the four. I taped the beam to the edge of the table, and applied a single coat to the beam, from bottom to top, one line at a time. I used my aluminum ruler to measure half an inch from the right edge of the panel, and marked it with the Micron. Then I filled in the space in the top right corner of the panel between the mark and the right edge of the letters. I then followed suit with the left edge, marking half an inch in and then filling in the empty space. The idea was that after the 4" beams cover the sides of the panel, all exposed areas of the panel will be non-blank.

> It's just after 5PM, and I have applied the first coat of nail polish to all four angle irons. However, I did have a little problem with the Mario beam. I started from the top and worked my way down. When I got to the 3D block, I noticed that the tip of the nail polish brush had a bit of light green on it. It turns out that the light green Sharpie ink doesn't like the nail polish, and melts when it comes in contact with it. So now, the top and left sides of the block are now badly smudged, but the block still looks like a 3D block, and so I'll leave it as it is. When I got to the Coins beam, I began by applying the polish around the outer parimeter of the light green part of the 3D block, and then covering the rest of the beam, leaving the light green uncovered. Because I now know the nail polish melts the light green Sharpie ink, I've decided to protect the side panels with the Polycrylic alone. Speaking of the side panels, I applied a tiny bit more black Micron ink to the sides of the top letter area of the left panel, in order to make sure everything that's not covered by the side 4" beams will be covered with ink.
