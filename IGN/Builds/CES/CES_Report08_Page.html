CES Report - Section 8:
3/16/2004 to 5/11/2004

5/11/2004 - During this morning's pre-dawn hours, I was once again browsing the Web, but this time trying my luck again with finding a PCI riser card which faces towards the motherboard. After using Google's Image Search, I came across a few right facing risers. The one I currently have my eye on is a one slot riser from LinITX.com. I can get it for five British pounds, but I'm not yet sure how much that will translate to in American dollars. My search for risers began making me think about how a typical PCI card would fit into CES with this riser in place. So I got one of my 3DFX Voodoo video cards I keep in storage, and compared it to an online picture of the VIA Epia V10000A motherboard I will order in the future. I found two potential problems that may get in the way later on in the project. First, the back end of a PCI card on a riser in CES will face towards the front panel, which means if the card is longer than seven inches, it won't fit in the chassis. The other concern is that when a PCI card is lying horizontally over the motherboard, part of it will hover over the RAM slots. However, I can avoid this problem if the riser card is high enough, and allows the PCI card to hover three inches over the motherboard.

> When I went to Memory4Less.com to check the height of the 1GB RAM stick I'm getting, I noticed that the price was jacked up to over $300. So it looks like I'll be yet again searching for a new RAM stick. Until I find a similar RAM stick for under $250 including S&H, I will leave that part of the budget unchanged. Also, I'm going to leave the riser card off the budget until I can get one that hovers higher over the motherboard, and won't run into the RAM.

5/9/2004 - Last night, I used a scale that my mother bought three days ago to weigh all the parts that I currently have of CES. At first, when I tried to weigh the parts on the scale, nothing registered. I soon realized that the scale doesn't work if whatever on it is below a certain weight, perhaps around ten or fifteen pounds. I was about to give up on the scale, but just then my parents got back home, and my mother suggested that I try a differential method of weighing the items, where I would first weigh myself, then weigh myself holding the items, and subtract the difference to get a weight on the items. When I first tried this method, I was expecting the items to weigh no more than eight pounds. I was shocked to learn that CES's parts weighed 10.2 pounds, a little more than my personal limit of ten pounds. (This weight included the spare side panel, the spare lexan panel, and the cover of my broken Maxtor.) So then I tried dividing the weight into three categories; chassis, drives, and everything else. While weighing the chassis, I put all the panels of the chassis, except for the spares, into the Puma shoebox. I included the plastic front panel, the hinges and knobs for my top hatch, but also the LEDs and switches, because I felt they were too flimsy to go with "everything else". The result came out to be around 5.4 pounds, way more that the 3.9 I was expecting. For the drives, I put my floppy drive into my small white shoebox, but also included the broken slimline CD drive, as well as the broken Maxtor with all of its parts. This time, the result was around 2.8 pounds. Finally, I weighed "everything else", which turned out to be the two fans and one fan mount, and two grills. Again, I was surprised at the weight, which this time was about 1.8 pounds. The total weight between the three categories: exactly ten pounds, the maximum weight limit.

> However, I don't entirely trust the scale, nor the results it was giving me. Every time I weighed something, I first stood on the scale empty-handed to see how much I weighed for comparison purposes. The scale gave me readings as low as 124 lbs, and as high as 128.6 lbs, but 126.4 seemed to be the standard. After I had weighed the three categories separately and added up the total, I then weighed all the items together, and came up with a weight of 9.6 lbs. After I had been using the scale for a while, I began to notice that it never displayed an odd tenth of a pound number, so that all the readings came out to be .0, .2, 4, etc. Today, I'm going to see if I can get CES's parts weighed at the scale in A&P's produce section.

> I naturally then started looking for ways to get CES to lose weight, and in future steps of the build, there will be plenty of opportunities to do this. Some of the angle irons will have bites cut out of them in order to accommodate certain other parts of the computer. The biggest example will be the seven inch beams at the top of the chassis, which will each have five square inches cut out of them in order to accommodate the top hatch hinges. Also, the bottom and back panels will each have a large circular hole cut out of them for CES's two fans. Finally, the top aluminum hatch doors still need to have their windows holes cut out of them. I'm hoping that this all this chopping down will give me some relief, but it may not be enough to get CES's final weight below ten pounds. I weighed everything conservatively, factoring in the weight of the boxes and individual bags some of the items were in, but this is only good for shaving off a few ounces at most from the final result.

> Desperate for more solutions, I checked my current budget to see if there were any items I could remove in order to save weight on CES. Out of all the items left to buy for CES, the 40mm fan and EL Cable seemed to be the heaviest. The slimline CD drive and hard drive had already been compensated for in the weighing, so I didn't consider those for elimination. I eventually decided to remove the green EL cable, since it's the one item that I'm least sure of how to get working right on CES, and it's the one item in which I can't estimate its weight, because I've never worked with such a cable before. So that's a $9.50 item that's coming off my budget. My computer won't glow as bright anymore, but at least now there won't be as much conflicting with the LED fan.

$725.99 total budget - $9.50 for Green EL cable = $716.49 total budget

> While surfing the Web for a slimline floppy or hard drive to replace the heavier IDE version on my budget, I came across some slimline hard drives on Directron.com, but none of them sold for less then a dollar a gigabyte, so I immediately gave up on that. I found a slimline floppy drive for only twenty dollars and was tempted to buy it, but something inside told me to first shop around some more. I went to NewEgg.com and ran a search for the word "floppy". The first result was quite a pleasant surprise. It was a pair of slimline drives sold as one item, one floppy, one CD/DVD drive. The pair sold for only $94.99, and better yet, it came with its own rack. I decided immediately to buy the pair, even though the CD drive isn't a CD burner, and I'll be paying around thirteen bucks more for the pair that for the Samsung CDRW/DVD drive I was going to get. However, despite these drawbacks, switching from the CDRW/DVD drive to the pair of drives will solve two key problems I'm having with my project. First, switching from my standard floppy drive to the slimline floppy drive will significantly reduce CES's weight, perhaps by as much as six ounces. This may not seem like much, but because CES is so close to the weight limit, I can use ALL the breaks I can get. Of course, this switch means I won't be using the floppy with that really cool yellow LED, so it will be a shame to se the work that JD and I did to change the LED go to waste. The second advantage this purchase offers is that it comes with its own drive rack, meaning I may not have to build one myself! (A major ass itch out of the way, for sure.) I will still have to find a way to attach the rack to the inside of the chassis, but I'll cross that bridge when I get to it. I don't know if I'll still need to buy a Slimline CD Desktop to IDE Adapter, so I'll order the drive rack first, and take the adapter off of the budget if it turns out that I don't need it. But until I can confirm I don't have any use for it, the adapter stays on the budget.

$716.49 total budget + $13.00 more spent = $729.49 total budget

$729.49 total budget - $2.00 less spent = $727.49 total budget

5/2/2004 - Just before six this morning, I began chopping down my piece of shit Veo Capture 1300. But before I started the disassembly, I removed the 32MB flash memory card, the CMOS battery, and and four AA batteries from the camera. When I removed the AAs, I noticed that two of them were corroded and / or leaking acid. Since they sure as hell weren't like that when I put them into the camera, I assumed that the camera somehow caused the leak. This of course made me all the more sure about disposing of the camera. I began the chop down by using a thin Phillips head screwdriver to unscrew five screws on various sides of the camera, and then pulling off the faceplate. My first concern was the safe removal of the 1" x 1 1/4" LCD screen, in the hopes that I might someday use it with the front panel of CES. After unscrewing the only screw that was holding it on, I noticed that three wires attached it to the camera's main board; a tiny white wire, a tiny pink wire, and a thin amber ribbon cable. I was able to remove the ribbon, but I accidentally broke a plastic piece which held it in place. However, I don't think I damaged the wire itself. Sometime in the future, I'll have to unsolder the pink and white wire from the main board, but until then, it will remain stuck to the board. I also noticed that the two LEDs I was after were encased in some kind of white plastic tubes, which may prevent me from unsoldering them from the main board, so I'll have to find a way to remove them without damaging the LEDs.

> When I unscrewed the mainboard from the backplate, I noticed that there was a third LED, which was red and larger than the other two. It was encased in the same type of plastic tube as the other two LEDs. After chopping down the rest of the camera and laying all the parts out on the table, I decided what parts to throw away, and which ones to keep. I salvaged the memory card, CMOS battery, a clear tiny plastic plate, LCD monitor, a camera-like object, the flashbulb with its capacitor, and all of the screws. Since I couldn't think of a potential use for any of the other parts, I threw them away. I wanted to keep the battery casing, but I was forced to throw it out because it was covered in battery acid.

4/26/2004 - Two days ago, while my parents and I were on our way back from Aventura, we stopped by the garage of my aunt Pat and uncle Garret in Virginia. Because their family is in the business of car restoration, I explained to my cousin Gary how I had used Sharpie markers to paint the flames onto the side panel of CES. I asked him if lacquer was a suitable protective coating, and he suggested it was, but I should use a separate piece of aluminum to test the lacquer on Sharpie ink before applying it to the real thing. He warned that the lacquer could cause the Sharpie ink to bubble and become distorted, so I'll take his advice and test the lacquer on my unused side panel. Around 8PM that night, my parents and I arrived at my brother Peter's house in Cherry Hill, New Jersey. During our overnight stay, I asked Peter about using lacquer on my panels. He suggested I use polyurethane instead of lacquer as a protective coat, but I forgot why he made this suggestion. So it looks like I'll be testing both coatings on my extra panel, and whichever one has the better effect is the one I'll use as the coating for my side panels and 4" angle irons, once all of the painting is done.

> I've been thinking a lot about finishing the design on my side panels lately. I already have the green flames painted onto the panels, but the top 20 millimeters of each panel is still unpainted. I've been saving this area for the name of the project, the Creamy Entertainment System, and for months I was having trouble with a final design. Over the past few days, I've come up with one. The panels are each going to have a different name design. One panel will have the words, "THE CREAMY ENTERTAINMENT SYSTEM" in 7 x 7 pixel, 1 x 1 centimeter letters in the classic NES font. The words will take up three rows, and will be aligned to the left side of the panel, however I'll have to set aside 20 millimeters on each side of the panel in order to accommodate the 4" angle irons. "THE CREAMY" will take up the first row, and "ENTERTAINMENT" will take up the second. In order to fit the word "SYSTEM" on the third row, I'll have to erase the flames on the top two rows of the existing pattern from the fifth to the tenth column. The other panel will have the words, "Jason "Creamy Goodness" Tenn's Creamy Entertainment System" in 1/2 x 1 centimeter letters in the Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past_font. The words will be divided into two rows; "Jason "Creamy Goodness" Tenn's" will take up the upper row, while "Creamy Entertainment System" will be on the bottom row. I will not have to erase any of the flames on this panel to make room for the letters.

> During this morning's pre-dawn hours, I made a slight design change to my Super Mario Bros. block design for my 4" angle irons. Now, only one edge of each beam will be painted instead of both, because the front edges will be covered up by the front panel, and the back edge of one of the back beams will have a chunk cut out of it in order to accommodate the rear fan and grill. This means that when my sprite aides are finished, I'll have to make 1 x 4 inch grids instead of 2 x 4 on paper to simulate the new design. I've already drawn sprite aides for three of the four beams; one with Mario, one with a Koopa Troopa, and the other with two coins.

> Early this morning, I decided to work on the design of the 4" angle irons which will rest vertically on each corner of CES. The beams will be decorated with Super Mario Bros graphics, so that each side of the outside of the beams will have four 1" squares stacked on top of each other. Each square will have a sprite or a block from SMB. In order to copy such graphics, I visited The Mushroom Kingdom for some references. I found a picture of Luigi in an underground area, and the picture contained a Goomba, two Koopa Troopas, three types of blocks, and some coins. The picture makes a perfect reference for my angle irons, and I plan to draw the graphics in green. When I zoomed into the image with Paint Shop Pro, I discovered that each block is 16 x 16 pixels in size.

> Originally, I was going to draw all for beams in the same way; a floor brick on the bottom, and three breakable bricks on top of those. But now, I think I'll add some variety to the design. The floor block will be at the bottom of each outer side, but the similarities between the sides will end there. Mario or Luigi will be on one of the beams, while another will have a Goomba, while another will have a Koopa Troopa. There will also be a variety of patterns of the blocks and coins throughout the beams. Before I start applying any paint to my aluminum beams, I'm going to make a scale simulation on paper, just like I did with the green flames. Because the blocks are 16 x 16 pixels, I can use my trusty ruler to make marks 1/16" apart on a sheet of printing paper, and then draw four 2 x 4 inch grids, each grid representing a beam. Once I come up with a final design for each of my beams, I will fill in the grids with colored pencils. I will then follow a similar procedure with the real beams, but I'll have to use fine-point markers to get the job done.
