2/3/2004 - Yesterday, received two packages in the mail. They were the items I ordered online a week ago; two hard drive coolers, and two green cold cathode fan grills. I was busy cleaning the house yesterday, so I had to wait until today to open the boxes. So far, I only opened the box with the fan grills, and they were a lot bigger than I imagined them to be. I decided to test one of the fans to see how well it would fit inside my chassis. I took one of the fans out of its mount, and felt confident that it was thin enough to fit OK. I compared it's depth to a box of toothpicks I'd been using as a stand-in measuring prop for my fan, and the fan was a lot thinner. I brought my chassis parts down to the kitchen, and assembled the chassis minus the top and side panels. I also brought down my IDE and Molex cords, plugged them into my broken Maxtor hard drive, and placed the drive where the real thing will go inside the chassis. When I put the fan in its place behind the hard drive, I didn't notice any problems. I then put the empty fan mount under the hard drive, but when I did that, the plugs kept knocking the back panel down, so I taped the panel and four-inch beams to the rest of the chassis temporarily. There was just enough room for the fan to miss passing in front of the Molex plug, just as I'd calculated through my experiments with the toothpick boxes. However, the IDE flat cord was just being a total bitch, constantly running into the fan, even though there was at least a full inch of space between them.

> Putting the hard drive on top of the fan mount made me wonder if there would be enough vertical space for the floppy drive and slimline CD drive to fit on top of the hard drive, and still allow the top hatch to fully close. So I stacked the floppy drive and CD drive on the hard drive, while the CD drive was sticking through its bay hole. There was barely enough room to accommodate all three drives, and there could be no more than zero clearance between them. So I put my two 12" top angle irons_under the front panel, raising it by a quarter-inch. I then used angle iron to separate the three drives, leaving an 1/8" inch of space between each one. When I then tried to adjust the drives horizontally, the hard drive slid off of its mount. So I screwed the drive onto the mount, and this made things a lot easier for me.

> I placed a seven inch beam vertically on the front-right corner of my chassis where a four inch beam would normally go. (I was using my two front four inch beams to give the floppy drive a 1/8" boost.) I then put the front panel in place with the CD drive in its bay, and slid it as far to the right as it would go until the CD drive touched the 7" beam. I lined up the right edges of the floppy and hard drives to match that of the CD drive's. After that, I put the fan back into place. I figured I could get the fan as close to the right edge of the back panel as possible without passing in front of the back-right corner vertical 4" beam, and have the only about the first millimeter of the fan to pass in front of the hard drive's Molex connector. Because the Molex would be plugged into the internal power supply, which will be placed in the opposite direction of the fan, the Molex cord will be pulled away from the fan, and not be an issue. I'm hoping the same will apply to the IDE cord, but it's a little more tricky than the Molex. I removed the CD drive, floppy drive, and front panel in order to make room for the ruler, so I could measure the distance between the hard drive and the fan. It turned out to be about an inch, while the back of the IDE connector was about 3/4" away from the fan. I don't know if this ultimately will be enough space for my IDE cord, but I'm sure I'll find out later on in the project.

> Just now, at 11:45PM, I discovered a bit of great news. I put the floppy and CD drives back into place without the beams separating them, put the front panel back over the CD drive, and the 4" beams back onto the front corners. When the front panel reached as right as the CD drive would allow it to go, it was exactly in between in corner beams. This means I may not have to move the front panel CD bay hole after all. 1/30/2004 - For the past week, I've been stumped on how to mount the two switches and two LEDs to the switch / LED mounting plate that will rest just behind the left side of my front panel. This morning, I came up with a few possible solutions to this problem. The plate will be mounted about a quarter inch behind the front panel. If I mount the reset button directly to it, the button will be too far behind the front panel to reach with your average index finger. When I first discovered this, I knew I would have to find some way of raising the button off the plate and closer to the front panel, but I could never figure out a good way to do this until recently. There will be two holes in the mounting plate for the reset button; one hole for the switch itself, and a smaller screw hole. I think that if I find a bottle cap that's just the right size, I can cut a hole in it the size of the switch, then glue it over the reset switch hole on the mounting plate. This will hopefully let the reset button stick through its hole in the front panel, and still be mounted solidly to the plate. But the button's screw hole will now be about a quarter inch away from its corresponding hole on the plate, so I'll need a longer screw to hold them together. The bottle cap's height will have to be the difference between the front of the reset button, and the back of the front panel in order to make an effective mount.

> I'm looking at my front panel right now, and I'm noticing that its two LED shades stick out of the back of the panel a little bit. This means my power and HDD LEDs would almost stick into their front panel shades if they were mounted directly to the mounting plate. However, this wouldn't be a good idea, because the metal surface of the plate would cause a short circuit in the LEDs and prevent them from functioning. So I'm going to have to mount the LEDs on a small and thin piece of plastic, and then attach it onto the plate. Finding such a mount isn't as easy as you think, because the requirements are very demanding. It can't be higher that a quarter inch, because that would go through the front panel. It can't be too wide, because it has to fit between the Schweppes bottle top and the bottom of the chassis. When I was at JD's house last weekend and about to solder the red LED to its motherboard cable, JD asked me if I wanted to cut the prongs down to size. And to make things still more complicated, I still don't know what part the front-bottom angle iron of my chassis is going to play in this. So the best I can do for now is just gather whatever plastic caps or other pieces of plastic I think may be a potential mount for my LEDs, fit my chassis together, and just experiment from there.

> Whooo, boy! I just got back from JD's house, and we managed to get quite a bit of work done on CES. > The first thing we did was solder the power LED and the HDD LED to my second motherboard cable. JD used a pair of pliers to cut the cable in half, and then stripped them with the same pliers. The stripping was far neater than when I tried to do the same thing over a month ago with the cable I used for my switches. Anyway, JD soldered the green LED that was going to be the power LED of CES to the first half of the motherboard cable, and showed me step by step the basics of soldering. Apparently, you have to first let the soldering iron heat up until it can melt solder. Then you have to melt a bit of solder with the iron, and as the solder melts, it has to cover both contact points (in this case, each LED prong and each wire), a process called tinning. You then twist each wire to a contact point, making sure there is as much contact between the two as possible. It's best if the contact points don't easily slide, and if the wire's twist gets to the end of the contact point. Finally, the soldering iron is used to solder the connections in place, and this causes the solder already on the points to melt and glue the points together. JD tinned the contacts for the clear-shaded red LED and the second half of the cable, but then I jumped in and asked to try the rest of the process first hand. JD suggested I attach the red wire to the LED prong closest to the flat side of the LED. (The flat side is almost impossible to see unless you're looking at it from the correct angle.) It took me a few attempts to twist the contact point right, but I think I did a decent job for a first try. I soldered the two contact points together, then repeated the process with the other two points. I was very excited about seeing my first LEDs wired and ready to go, but I have yet to test them on Polyfragger.

He had me use a large drill bit to find the center of the power LED cut circle, and when I felt confident that I had, he took over and hit the back of the drill bit with some big wooden mallet several times until the hole poked through the back of the panel. He thought the best way to drill was through the back, because the protrusions in the back of the panel were uneven, and prevented the panel from being laid down firmly on its back. So JD use his pair of pliers to take the border_off the panel, and this made it easier to work with. Then JD drilled the hole for the power LED, and I was immediately amazed at how easily the drill chewed through the front panel's plastic. I spent several days cutting and chiseling some holes in my front panel making several mistakes and cracking the panel, and JD uses this drill to do the same thing in seconds, making a perfect circle without the cracks. But the hole was too small, because we decided to drill conservatively. So he then measured the circumference of the sawed-off lipstick cap that was to be my power LED shade, using a slightly bigger drill bit to make the correction, and then the cap fit perfectly in the hole.

> The same process was repeated with the Schweppes bottle top, but the bottom of it was much wider than the point where it connected to its widest ring. Then, JD had what I consider to be a stroke up genius. He took the top and panel upstairs, and at first I had no idea what for.
