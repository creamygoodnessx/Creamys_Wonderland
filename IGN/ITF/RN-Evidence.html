(From page 13) - Individuals more suited to their environment have a better chance to survive to reproductive age than their less favorably endowed counterparts. Creatures that survive pass their favorable features genetically to future generations, while those less suited die out. This is called Natural Selection.
- (p. 16) - "Fitness" is determined by how well a species survives in its environment, and not necessarily by the physical strength, speed, or ferocity of its individuals.
- (p. 16) - Survival is not necessarily determined by competition. Group cooperation, and even cooperation between species, can determine the species' fitness.

(From page 18) - Sometimes, genes go through a spontaneous change called a mutation. Most mutations are so small, they can't normally be detected. Some rare genetic mutations produce creatures that can't survive in their environment.
- (p. 18) - Genetic mutations can be passed on to future generations. If certain mutations are favorable for a species, individuals with those mutations will be more likely to survive to reproduce than those without.
- (p. 18) - Each favorable genetic mutation is usually too small to notice, but the results become apparent as the number of mutations accumulate through several generations.

- (p. 19) - Geochemical dating suggests the Earth is about 5 billion years old.
- (p. 20) - Organic materials of living matter are created when carbon dioxide, methane, and ammonia are exposed to high amounts of electricity and ultra-violet radiation. The first organic materials are the result of certain conditions during the formation of the Earth. During this time, the Earth's crust was still molten rock, and intense volcanic activity released carbon dioxide, methane, and ammonia into the atmosphere.. The extreme heat of the atmosphere produced electrical storms. Because there was no ozone layer, UV radiation from the sun bombarded the planet.

(From page 20) - After the Earth's crust cooled, it rained continuously for millions of years, creating the planet's oceans. These oceans had a high concentration of of organic molecules, about 0.1% to 1% of the ocean's total molecules. This concentration is sometimes called the "primeval soup" or "primordial soup".

(From Page 23) - Evidence of evolution comes from two sources; the living world and the fossil record.

(From Page 24) - The taxonomic level at which two species is compared depends on the features being considered.
- (p. 24) Domestic dogs and wolves have the same basic skeletal structure, reproduce the same way, have similar dental patterns, and are genetically compatible. Most of the differences between the two species are superficial, so they belong to the same genus(Canis).
- (p. 24) - Because the red fox is a dog-like creature just like the domestic dog or wolf, all three belong to the dog family(Canidae). However, the red fox is in a different genus(Vulpes) from the domestic dog and wolf, because of more significant differences between them, such as in dental and skeletal structure.
- (p. 24) - Bears(the family of Ursidae) and Canidae are all primarily carnivores, so they all belong in a single order, Carnivora. However, bears are obviously different from all dog-like forms.
- (p. 25) Species are separated at the level of species if their differences are not adaptive to their environment, but are only the result of chance mutations.
- (p. 25) - The more strongly the adaptive features being considered when comparing two species, the higher the taxonomic level from which they are split.

(From page 28) - If two groups of individuals of a species become geographically isolated, they can no longer exchange genetic materials. This is called "reproductive isolation". Since genetic mutations are random, it is unlikely the two groups will have the same mutations, and differences will begin to appear between them as a result. The randomness that causes these differences is known as "genetic drift".
- (p. 28) - If this process happens for enough generations, the two groups may eventually become separate species. Any differences in the environmental conditions of the two groups will speed up the process, because of natural selection.
- (p. 33) - Should the geographical barriers between the two groups disappear, and the two groups begin exchanging genetic materials again, and any differences that were the result of the genetic drift will be diffused.

- In a sentient species, some religions forbid intermarriage between members of other religions. This could be the case in Evil Blood, where in some of Nnet Olotor's religions, the penalty for such intermarriage is death. If this policy were to be successfully enforced for enough generations, those whose practiced it would become increasingly different from those outside their religion, because of genetic drift. After thousands of generations, members of the religion would become noticeably different from the rest of the species, and would become a separate species altogether.

(From Page 29) - Since genetic change is random, several genetic variations often exist within a species. If the species occupies a large geographical area, local variants will have their own distinct characteristics. This is because individuals in one area are far more likely to mate with members of surrounding areas than with members of more distant areas. These local groups can be considered to be sub-species of the species. When an individual mates with a neighbor of a distant group, their offspring will have traits of both local groups. Therefore, individuals with characteristics from multiple groups exist, erasing any clear dividing lines between the sub-species. The species is then considered to be polytypic.
- (p. 30) - If a species is spread out over a large geographical area, its members may be able to overcome geographical barriers and prevent genetic isolation if they share three characteristics...
-----------   - The species must be able to travel long distances easily. Being large helps.
-----------   - The species must be able to survive in a variety of environments.
-----------   - The members of the different areas must be tolerant of each other as far as breeding goes.
- (p. 37) - If one local group of a species stops breeding with outside groups, then intergrades of the species will disappear. This usually happens as the result of geographic isolation. At this point, a species is usually recognized as a separate species from the original group.

(From page 39) - Polar bears began evolving from brown bears at the start of the last ice age, over 10,000 years ago. The increasing cold and shrinking food supply killed off most of the brown bears in the northern areas of the Old World. In order to survive, the few surviving brown bears turned to the ocean for food. Here they became geographically isolated from the rest of the brown bear population. Only the bears who were best able to cope with the arctic environment survived to pass on their genes to further generations. As conditions grew worse, natural selection forced these generations to adapt features that helped the bears survive in the arctic sea. Eventually, the polar bears became dependent on their new environment for survival. When the glaciers began to retreat, and the brown bears returned, the polar bears could no longer compete, and were forced to travel north.

- There could be a point in the story of Evil Blood where a large group of people was forced out of their homeland, and had to live in a far less hospitable environment. This could be the result of persecution on a large scale, or a natural disaster. Most of the people died as a result of the harshness of the environment, but those who survived were able to pass their more favorable genetic traits down to their descendants. This could have caused the people to evolve into a new species from their original one.

(From Page 63) - Life on Earth began in the ocean. Most terrestrial life made their way into fresh water through rivers, and then traveled onto land.
- (p. 66) - Some ocean life-forms came to land by way of the tidal zone (through beaches).

(From Page 66) - Since all life depends on green plants as the ultimate food source, in order for animals live in a particular area, green plants must first thrive there. After plant-eaters settle in the area to eat the plants, the meat-eaters soon follow and prey on the plant-eaters.
- (p. 66) - The tiny plant life that lived in the ocean was picked up by violent storms and water spouts, and many were deposited in fresh water environments (lakes, rivers, etc.).
- (p. 67) - Once the tiny plant life had established itself in fresh water, it attracted the plant-eaters. But in order for the plant-eaters to reach the fresh water, they had to travel up the rivers. Some of the creatures that could do this were the ones that walked on the ocean floor, because they could fight the rivers' current.
- (p. 67) - Once in the creatures settled in fresh water, they evolved features that helped them to better survive in that environment, such as tolerance of lower salinity.
- (p. 69) - Because the first fresh water creatures had no competition, they easily thrived in their new environment.

(From Page 73) - Cephalopods (octopus, squid, etc.) were so successful at life in the ocean that they did not have the incentive to travel into fresh water, even though they were preadapted for life in that environment.

(From Page 75) - Jaws evolved from the breathing mechanism of early jawless fish, the agnaths. The jaws of all living vertebrates are operated by the same nerves as the front pairs of respiratory gill bars of the agnaths. As the agnaths grew larger, they needed a way to draw in more oxygen to accommodate for their size. This led to the mobility of the creatures' gill bars, which soon had the capability of clamping onto prey. The gill bars then were converted into grasping jaws, and grew tiny teeth, making catching prey even easier.

(From Page 84) - The single most important preadaptive feature a sea creature needs to adapt to life on land is a form of lung.
- (p. 84) - Ancient fossils of placoderms, an extinct form of early fish, had a "blind pouch". This pouch branches off from the throat, and is where the lungs would be if it were a land creature.
- (p. 84) - Many of today's fish have a "swim bladder", a gas filled bag that helps the fish control buoyancy, and in some species of fish, aids in respiration. In fish embryos, the swim bladder is in the same position as the lungs in the embryos of land vertebrates. These swim bladders evolved into lungs after the fish began spending time on land.

(From Page 85) - The lobe-finned fish gave rise to the first tetrapods, which are any creatures that travel on four legs.
- (p. 88) - The lobe-fins were pre-adapted for life on land, because they already had a certain organ that was capable of breathing air.
- (p. 88) - The lobe-fins first used their fins on land to crawl from pool to pool as the pools evaporated. Any characteristics that made this land trip easier were strongly selected for, and thus the lobe-fins developed features for traveling on land.
-----------   - Small size was initially favored, because this allows the fish to require less skeletal support. However, larger size eventually became favored, because it allowed the fish to travel longer distances on land.
-----------   - The lobe-fins evolved greater flexibility, because walking on land requires greater flexibility than swimming does.
-----------   - The lobe-fins' fins became longer and stronger in order to make walking easier.
-----------   - The lobe-fins fed on arthropods while traveling between pools. This was their motivation for remaining on land, and allowed them to give rise to land vertebrates.

(From Page 95) - No organism on Earth is completely independent of water. Creatures that migrate to dry land can only live there because they know how to take advantage of what little water they can find.

(From Page 96) - Cold blooded creatures, such as reptiles, depend on the temperature of the environment to regulate their own body temperature. They raise their body temperature by sitting in the sun, and lower it by hiding in the shade.
- (p. 96) Pelycosauria, an order under the reptile class, had a sail-shaped body. When they needed to warm up, they stood so that the sun would hit them broadside. When they needed to cool down, they turned toward the sun, which minimized the amount of sunlight hitting their bodies.

(From Page 97) - In order to conserve water, modern lizards and birds excrete their waste as nearly dry pellets or as slurry. The diapsids, the creatures that eventually evolved into lizards and birds, also had this water conserving feature, which allowed them to follow their insect prey into drier areas. The diapsids no longer had to look for water, because they got all the moisture they needed from their food.

(From Page 98) - The more body mass (volume) a creature has, the faster it will gain heat. The more surface area a creature has, the faster it will lose heat.
- (p. 98) The larger dinosaurs cooled off very slowly. Because their muscles were so big, they also generated a lot of body heat. This put them at risk of overheating.
-----------   - One method animals can use to avoid overheating is panting. However, large dinosaurs were too large for panting to be efficient. Instead, they seeked the shelter of large bodies of water to cool themselves off.

(From Page 104) - Insectivores are the earliest of the advanced mammals.
- (p. 104) - Although insectivores feed primarily on insects, they also include vegetation in their diet.
- (p. 104) - Because insectivores are small creatures, they can easily hide from predators, and don't have elude them by running fast over long distances.
- (p. 104) - Insectivores have flat, five-toed feet that have some grasping ability.
- (p. 105) - Insectivores have a five-fingered hand with an opposable thumb. When some of the insectivores found their way into the trees, they developed these grasping hands and eventually gave rise to primates.
- (p. 105) - One disadvantage insectivores suffer because of their small size, is that they lose heat very rapidly. In order to stay warm, they must eat constantly, and consume several times their weight in food. The insectivores' descendants that became larger, including rodents and primates, no longer had this problem.

- The gwahrai race is heavily influenced by insectivores and early primates, and has many of their physical features. The gwahrai are adapted for a life of climbing trees, gathering fruits, and eluding predators. They have three long fingers and an opposable thumb on both their hands and their feet. This was originally developed to make it easier for them to grab branches and pick up food, but it also came in handy for using weapons. The gwahrai once eluded predators by hiding high in the trees, and could easily outrun most of them as long as they stayed in the trees. But eventually, they began to actively defend themselves against such predators, fighting back with whatever they could use as a weapon. The gwahrai's diet consists mainly of vegetation, although they also feed on insects when they get the chance.
