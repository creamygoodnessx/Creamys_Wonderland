(From Page 43) - Spontaneous generation means that a life form originates from non-living matter. However, this does not necessarily mean that a lifeform appears instantaneously, it only means that it appears unaided by any previous life, regardless of the amount of time the lifeform takes to appear.

(From Page 43) - In the early 1920's, Russian biochemist A. I. Oparin first came up with the theory about the origins of life that is widely accepted today. He suggested that the first living cells formed gradually as the result of certain chemical reactions over a very long period of time. In 1928, a similar theory about the origins of life was proposed by English biochemist J. B. S. Haldane. His theory was that the first living cells were created when the mixture of water vapor, ammonia, methane, and hydrogen were exposed to the ultra-violet light from the sun. Although their theory, the Oparin-Haldane hypothesis, is still widely followed today, many modifications have been made to it since its creation.
- (p. 48) - According to the original Oparin-Haldane hypothesis, there was little or no oxygen in the atmosphere in prebiotic times (before the existence of life) around 3 billion years ago. Since oxygen destroys living compounds in a process called oxidation, an oxygen-free environment should have made it possible for organic compounds to mix and create the first living cells. However, many scientists now believe that there may have been significant amounts of oxygen back then, creating a problem for the Oparin-Haldane hypothesis.

(From Page 43) - According to the original Oparin-Haldane hypothesis, the first living cells were created high up in the atmosphere when the mixture of water vapor, ammonia, methane, and hydrogen were exposed to certain energy sources, such as ultra-violet light, and electrical storms. This led to the formation of the first organic materials on Earth. When these organic compounds gathered in the ocean after their formation, they mixed and reacted with each other to form more complicated substances, such as proteins, DNA, and sugar molecules. As the process continued, more and more complex chemicals were formed, and this process eventually led to the first living cells.
- (p. 49) - The energy that builds up complex chemicals, such as amino acids, from simpler ones is also the energy that breaks them down again. If the complex chemicals are to be preserved, they would have to reach and be dissolved by the ocean waters. However, since these chemicals are formed way up in the atmosphere most of them would be destroyed before they could reach the ocean.

(From Page 44) - The first living cells were anaerobic, which means they could survive without oxygen. They were also heterotrophic, meaning they were unable to produce their own food from inorganic materials.

(From Page 52) - When certain amino acids join together in a nitrogen atmosphere at certain temperatures, they can form long chains of amino acids called "proteinoids". These proteinoids are not true proteins, but contain some of their properties. If proteinoids are dissolved in boiling water which is then cooled, they will clump together in a spherical form. It's believed by some scientists that these "proteinoid microspheres" can eventually develop into cells.
- (p. 53) - Cells are highly complex biological machines, and are far more complex than proteinoid microspheres. A single DNA molecule of any given cell contains about 60 different types of proteins, and if even one of these proteins is missing, the cell will not create new proteins. A cell is so complex, that it probably could not be created by the chance mixing of random proteins, even given billions of years.
- (p. 55) -   - Because of the high level of improbability that cells could be generated by the random mixing of chemicals, some scientists believe that the first cells were created from the design of some outside, intelligent force.

(From Page 59) - Charles Darwin initially believed that all of the traits within a given creature were the blended result from both of its parents. If a creature had a particular trait, it would be lost in future generations because of the blending. This theory was eventually proven false.
- (p. 60) - The studies of Gregor Mendel proved that the traits of a given creature are preserved as they pass from generation to generation. This is because the traits of two parents, rather than mixing in their offspring, are kept separate. The offspring exhibits only one of these traits (the dominant trait), while the other remains hidden (the recessive trait). Both of these traits is then passed onto further generations.

(From Page 64) - During the late 19th Century, it was believed that common traits in a gene pool were caused by common genes and became increasingly common, and less common traits were caused by less common genes and were becoming less common. However, it was later discovered that common traits were caused by dominant genes, and less common traits were the result of recessive genes. Such genes were as common as they were in one generation as they were in past generations.
- (p. 65) - Genetic mutations can reduce the number of a particular gene in a gene pool, and can result in new genes.
- (p. 66) -   - Most genetic mutations are detrimental to a given species, and include genetic diseases. Only 1 in 1000 of such mutations can benefit the species.

(From Page 75) - The more surface area a creature has, the faster it will lose heat. The more body mass (volume) a creature has, the faster it will gain heat.
-----------   - Slim creatures with long, slender limbs have a higher surface to volume ratio and will cool off faster. Fatter creatures with short limbs have a lower surface to volume ratio and will cool off more slowly. This is why creatures in the arctic tend to have fat bodies and short limbs, while creatures that live in hot climates are thin and have long, thin limbs.

(From Page 78) - It's possible for a phylum to contain only one species. If this is the case, the phylum is considered the same as the species.

(From Page 79) - Genetic change in a species is more likely to take place in small populations than in larger ones.
- (p. 80) - Members of small populations are usually forced to interbreed very closely. When a genetic mutation occurs in such a population, it's more likely to spread throughout the entire population than in a larger population. Genetic mutations are likely to accumilate in the species in a small population, rather than spreading itself thin and disappearing like they would do in larger populations.

(From Page 82) - If a species is spread out over a very large geographical area, many variations of the species can exist. Members in one area will probably be only slightly different from its surrounding neighbors, because a local population is more likey to interbreed with its immediate neighbors than with any other populations. This interbreeding keeps neighboring populations from genetically drifting too far from each other. However, because populations that live on opposite edges of the geographical area don't normally get a chance to exchange genetic material, random genetic mutations can cause them to drift apart over time. Eventually, the two populations can become genetically incompatable. However, they will still be considered to be part of the same species, because of their ability to mate with their intermediates. However, if those intermediates should somehow disappear, the two surviving, incompatable populations can then be classified as two seperate species.
- (p. 84) - Reproductive isolation can also happen when a single environment contains varying geological conditions, such as different food sources, elevation, and temperature. A single species living in such a diverse environment can inhabit several different parts of it. Each of the species' populations will adapt to whatever conditions they live in, because natural selection will promote characteristics that will help them to survive under such conditions. The populations in each of the geological niches can become genetically isolated from each other if the genetic changes significantly differ from population to population.
