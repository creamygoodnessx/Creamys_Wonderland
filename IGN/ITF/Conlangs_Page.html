The constructed languages of Evil Blood

> This is the section where I will post the future Nnet Olotorian languages of Evil Blood. I've finally reached a point where I feel comfortable attempting another conlang since Xiao... can't remember and couldn't care less. This time around, I plan on trying to develop my conlangs naturally, instead of just assuming a bunch of random syllables will suffice. In other words, I'm going to start each language knowing generally what I want it to sound like, and will pretend that the very concept of spoken language doesn't exist. I will then build the language gradually, trying to have its development follow the same patterns a real developing language would. Some languages may eventually develop into written forms, or even lead to the creation of new languages.

> The following is an experimental phonic word list that will eventually lead to Nnet Olotor's most common language. None of these words have any meanings yet, nor is the grammar established; this is just my way of trying to get a feel of what the language will eventually sound like. I will start actually developing this language into something usable once I get a better understanding of how real languages are formed. Such research will take the form of reading books on language development, visiting conlang websites, and may even involve learning a second language. The Xs in the list are meant to sound like a soft Phoenician cheth sound, and the Cs are pronounced as 'ch'.

10/2/2003 - The primary elven language may have originated as one that referred to everything as masculine or feminine, much like Latin does. Such a language would most likely be spoken in a society where there is a significant gap in the roles of men and women. The people who spoke that language eventually colonized foreign lands, and in time, each colony developed their own dialect of the language, and in some cases, formed new languages. The colonists of one particular society may have had a civil rights movement that narrowed the social gap between the genders. In the later stages of this movement, the people may have developed gender neutral versions of their words and phrases, and gender specific words may have become politically incorrect. A civil rights movement may have only been one of many factors that caused the people's language to become completely separate from its original form.
