Characters

10/11/2001 - I've made a modification to my process of disigning the look of my characters. The steps are still in the same order as they were before, but I changed my technique on Steps 2 and 3. Before I make the height and details drawing, I draw a graph of guidelines for the character on an 8 1/2" x 11" sheet of printing paper. There are three types of such guidelines. The first and most important type of guidelines are the head markers, which are horizontal lines that measure the height of the character in heads from the top of the head (,excluding head accessories, such as hair or hats) to the floor. They are drawn a little bolder than the other types of lines. In my drawing style, characters are almost always drawn to the half head, so that they're excatly or almost exactly 3, 3 1/2, or 4 etc., heads tall. The second type of lines are the feature markers. These horizontal lines measure the vertical position of the character's features that do not rest on the head markers, and like the head markers, more than one feature may share a feature marker. These markers are more widely used on facial features than anywhere else on the body. I expect that two characters sharing the same set of feature markers will be a very rare occurrence. The third type of lines are the center lines, which are five vertical lines evenly spaced on the page.  The character is drawn on top of the line, so it passes through the character straight down the center. Center lines serve two purposes. First, they keep the character's features that are supposed to be in the center in the center, so the character stands straight up and doesn't slant to one side. Second, the center lines keep the five character drawings of the turnaround spaced evenly apart, so they don't overlap each other.

> After the graph is completed, I'm ready to start the height and details drawing. I place a sheet of tracing paper over the graph, and tape it on securely. I then draw the front, side, and back of the character on the first, third, and fifth center lines respectively, leaving in all the lines I used to draw the character. As I'm drawing, I use a millimeter ruler in order to make sure that both sides are drawn evenly. After the character is drawn , I write in the details of how I draw the character where the in-between angles would have been on the second and fourth lines. After signing and dating the drawing, I then scan it while it's still taped to the graph, completing the height and details step of the process.

> The next step of designing the appearance of my character is the turnaround. I start by placing a second sheet of tracing paper over the one I used to draw the height and details drawing, and again, taping it on securely. I then trace over the three drawings of the character, but this time omitting the unnecessary lines, giving the character a more 'finished' look. On the second and fourth center lines, I draw the in-between angles of the character, and erasing the unnecessary lines to give these too, a finished look. I sign and date the turnaround,  detatch it from the other two sheets, then tape it to a blank sheet of printing paper. Finally, I scan the turnaround.

> I used to draw the height and details drawing on a sheet of printing paper, then draw the turnarounds separately. I would use the graph and tracing paper technique on the turnarounds alone. I feel the new process is better, because it's easier to trace the front, side, and back angles from the h&ds, rather than re-draw them from scratch. Also, my h&ds are more detailed with three angles than they are with just the front, and I'm willing to draw three angle h&ds because of the convenience of only having to trace the turnarounds on top of them. I'm already drawing Stardancer's turnaround, but I drew her h&d using the old method, so I'll have to retrofit the turnaround with the new method. As I use this new design method on Oinkillune, I'll work on an illustrated version of the process, and give it its own section on IWOW.

7/3/2001 - I've come up with a five step system for me to follow whenever designing the appearance for my characters. This system is my own version of commonly followed techniques that professional cartoon animators use in their work.

Step 1: Concept sketch - Just a sketch of what I want the character to look like. The final version of the chracter can be dramatically different from what I originally intended him or her to look like. Concept sketches are sometimes based on one or more characters from other cartoons. For example, I used Alice Tompkins from The Critic as the base for Oinkillune. The concept sketch can depict either the whole body or just the head and shoulders, and can be drawn at any angle.

Step 2: Height and details - This is a drawing of the character's front view in his or her default pose, broken down into simple shapes with a few details. Important details about the character are pointed out, such as where certain parts of the body are located. This drawing also measures the character in heads. In my drawing style, a child is usually 2 and a half heads tall, adults are 3 and a half to 5 heads tall, while teens are usually somewhere in the middle.

Step 3: Turnaround - The character is drawn at five angles in the default pose; the front, the side, the back, and the two in-between angles. If the going gets rough in this sketch, I can model a Sculpey head of the character to make things a little easier.

Step 4: Movement sketch - Several quick sketches of the character in various poses. It's the final confirmation that the character is drawable from all angles.

Step 5: Character sheet - The character's official full color sheet, with the character in a dynamic pose, and a short list of his or her stats on the side. There will also be a short description of the character somewhere on the page.
