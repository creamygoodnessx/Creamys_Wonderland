My Quake2 Modeling Tutorial:
Step 2: Animations

> After your model's mesh is completed, and the bones are correctly applied, the next step is to animate the model for use in the game. A fully animated Quake2 plugin player model has 20 different animations. Each of the model's animations should be created in it's own Action, and have the correct number of frames. Once all 20 animations are completed, you can then complile them in a single choreography.

FRAMES LIST

> A Quake2 plugin player model consists of 20 individual animations which have a total of exactly 198 frames. Each animation has its own number of frames, ranging from 4 to 40. Some of the animations loop, so that they look like a continuous and unbroken animation when played repeatedly. When animating a PPM, it is mandatory that each animation consists of the correct number of frames, and loops where appropriate. In order to help you along, you can follow the Frames List on Polycount. If you for some reson you can't access the list, or have trouble following it, you can use my version of the list below...
 
 

Jason "Creamy Goodness" Tenn's Quake2 Frames List
Animation # of Frames Description Type
Stand 40 The Stand animation is the one that is most likely going to be seen more than any other animation, and therefore must be of especially high quality. At 40 frames, it is by far the longest of the animations, taking up over a fifth of the PPM's total frames. A character going through the Stand is most likely to be looking around. However, the animation can also be in the form of a fighting stance, or whatever else you can think of. You can be very creative with Stand by adding a few extras, such as having your character cock the gun, crack his/her knuckles, or scratch his/her head, but don't go for anything too flashy. Since Stand is very commonly seen, it can get old quickly, and I recommend that you save the true pizzaz for the less common animations. Stand is one of the four looping animations, which means its final frames should show the character smoothly returning to his/her state in the first frame. Loop
Run 6 Another looping animation, the Run plays while the character moves about the arena of death. The Run is a six frame animation that typically involves the first three frames dealing with one leg hitting the ground, while the other three frames are a mirror image of the first three. While making this animation, it's important to avoid "skating", or when your model's feet slide unnaturally along the floor when they're supposed to be planted in one place. After I finish the run animation for my models, I go to both the left or right view and the front or back view of my model, and zoom in on the planted foot in both views. I then run the animation one frame at a time, and adjust the foot so that it's on the ground in each frame. I then of course repeat the process with the other foot. Loop
Attack 8 This is the animation you'll see when the character fires his/her weapon. The weapon is discharged during the first frame of the Attack animation. I would recommend having the PPM in full recoil in the first frame, and then spend the rest of Attack recovering (returning to the first frame of Stand). Or, you could also put the recoil in the first frame, then have your character cock the gun quickly before returning to the first frame of Stand. Standard
Pain1 4 One of three animations played randomly when the character suffers damage. Standard
Pain2 4 One of three animations played randomly when the character suffers damage. Standard
Pain3 4 One of three animations played randomly when the character suffers damage. Also, the Change Weapon animation is Pain3 played backwards. Standard
Jump 6 The Jump animation is shown whenever the PPM becomes airborne. During a standard jump, the first two frames show the model reaching the peak of his/her jump, the third frame holds as long as the PPM stays in the air, while the last three frames show the landing. The third frame of Jump is your standard airborne frame and will hold until the PPM lands. If the PPM falls far enough to take damage, a random Pain animation will play instead of the last three frames of Jump. Standard
Flipoff 12 One of five gestures the character can make. It's usually used to tell the opposing players to fuck off. Standard
Salute 11 One of five gestures the character can make. Players can use this gesture as a sign of respect for their teamates. Standard
Taunt 17 One of five gestures the character can make. It's usually used to tell the opposing players to fuck off. Standard
Wave 11 One of five gestures the character can make. Also the Grenade Toss animation is Wave played backwards. You can opt to dedicate this animation to the Grenade Toss, but it can make Wave look awkward when actally used as a wave. Standard
Point 12 One of five gestures the character can make. This is when the character points straight ahead. Standard
Crouch Stand 19 This shows the PPM crouching, kneeling, or otherwise reducing his/her overall height. I recommend keeping your PPM at half normal height while using the Crouch animations.  Loop
Crouch Walk 6 The PPM walks while crouching. He/she becomes a smaller target, but also moves a lot more slowly than he/she would with a standard Run. The Crouch Walk can also take the form of a crawl. Loop
Crouch Attack 9 The PPM fires his/her weapon while crouching. Standard
Crouch Pain 4 The PPM takes damage while crouching. Standard
Crouch Death 5 The character dies while crouching. The character starts in his/her crouching position, and ends up lying on the ground. There are only five frames in this animation, so keep it as simple as you can. Death
Death1 6 The character starts in his/her standing position, and ends up lying on the ground.  Death
Death2 6 The character starts in his/her standing position, and ends up lying on the ground.  Death
Death3 8 The character starts in his/her standing position, and ends up lying on the ground. This animation consists of slightly more frames than the other Deaths, and allows a little more flexability. Death

ANIMATION TYPES
Standard - Begins and ends with the first frame of Stand or Crouch Stand.
Loop - Runs smoothly in a continuous cycle, so that the last frames lead up to the first frame.
Death - Begins with the first frame of Stand or Crouch Stand, but ends with the model's final, dead position.

LOOPING

> Aa any proficient animatior knows, looping is when an animation runs over and over again, A successful looping animation has its last frames blend smoothly into its first frame, so you can't tell exactly where the animation begins and ends. The Stand, Run, Crouch Stand, and Crouch Walk animations all loop. In order to create an animation that loops smoothly, simply follow these steps...

Create your animation normally, until you get to the final keyframe.

Go to the first keyframe, and while in Key Model Mode , copy the frame to the clipboard.

In the frame number text box, enter a number that is one frame higher than the intended number of frames in your animation.

Making sure you're still in Key Model Mode, paste the first frame of your model into your new keyframe.

While still in Key Model Mode, make the second-to-last frame in your animation a keyframe.

Delete the last frame in the animation (the frame you copied from the first frame). Your animation will now loop smoothly.

DUAL PURPOSE ANIMATIONS

> The Grenade Toss and Change Weapon animations are not officially included in the frames list. Instead, the grenade toss is Wave played backwards, while Change Weapon is Pain3 played backwards. If you want to create your PPM to specifically take advantage of the Genade Toss animation, simply create an Action for GT, animate it as usual (making sure GT has exactly 17 frames, of course), then reverse the keyframes, so that the animation runs backwards. Here's my method of reversing the frames in an animation...

After completing the Grenade Toss animation, create a new Action, and position its window next to one of the windows of your Grenade Toss animation.

While in Key Model Mode , copy the last keyframe of the Grenade Toss. Paste it onto the new action's first frame.

Count the number of steps between the last and second-to-last keyframe of the Grenade Toss. (The number of steps will be the number of frames between two keyframes, plus one. This is the number of frames you need to advance to get from one frame to another. For example, two back-to-back keyframes will have one step between them, and keyframes which are seven frames apart will have eight steps between them.) Copy the second-to-last keyframe.

Add the number of steps between the last and second-to-last keyframe of the Grenade Toss to the number of the current keyframe in the new action, which in this case will be 1. Enter the sum in the frame number text box. For example, if there are four steps between the last and second-to-last keyframe of the Grenade Toss, and the last keyframe you pasted in the new animation was 5, 4 + 5 = 9, so you would enter "9" in the frame number text box.

Paste the keyframe to the new frame of the new action.

Count the number of steps between the second-to-last and third-to-last keyframe of the Grenade Toss. Copy the third-to-last keyframe.

Add the number of steps between the second-to-last and third-to-last keyframe of the Grenade Toss to the number of the current keyframe in the new action. Enter the sum in the frame number text box, then paste the keyframe to the new frame of the new action. Repeat until the new animation is a reversed version of the Grenade Toss. If the new animation has exactly 17 frames, then you've done everything right.

COLLECTIVE CHOREOGRAPHY

> Once all 20 of your PPM's animations are finished, the next step is to compile them into a single choreography.
 

Start a new choreography.

Apply your model to the choreography. In the project workspace drag your model to the newly created choreography.

Import your PPMs Actions to the PPM in the choreography one by one. You must inport the Acions in the exact order of the frames table. (First Stand, then Run, then Attack, etc.) If you've imported all of the Actions and realise that one or more is out of order, you can correct this by draging the out-of-place Actions in their correct positions.

> Check the choreography to see if it has exactly 198 frames from the first frame of Stand, to the last frame of Death3. If there are more than 198 frames, either some of your Actions have extra frames, or you accidentally imported an Action twice. If there less than 198 frames, some of your Actions may have too few frames, or you may have forgotten to include an Action or two in your choreography. And of course, remember to save your choreography.
