QuakeCon 2012 - Day One

> I flew into Dallas Fort Worth from Philadelphia, and the plane landed around 8:45AM Dallas time. From there, I boarded a Super Shuttle bus at 9:15AM, and arrived at the Hilton Anatole which was to start hosting the QuakeCon of 2012 half an hour later. Once inside the hotel, I went right up to the front desk and registered my room, which turned out to be Room 574 in the Atrium, and then went to the Business Center to pick up the boxes containing my computer that I named The Creamy Entertainment System and its monitor, but the person behind the desk told me that they weren't going to arrive at the hotel until 10:30AM. My plan then was to drop off my suitcase in my hotel room, wait in line until 10:30PM, ask someone to hold my place in line, pickup CES and its monitor, then get back in line. When I got to my room, I transferred my Saitek illuminated keyboard from my suitcase to my backpack, so I could register my keyboard without having to take the suitcase in the line with me. I was about to leave the room to go wait in line when my mother called and said that the boxes were already there, and were signed for by someone called Mr. Spears. So I picked up and signed for both boxes, then went to the back of the BYOC line sometime before 10:30AM.

> When I first got in line, I was expecting to wait for less than an hour before getting to the registration desks in the Pre-Function Area of the Trinity building, signing in, and getting into the BYOC in the Trinity Exhibit Hall. However, the line turned out to be longer than any I have ever waited in at any of the previous QuakeCon events I've attended. During previous events, the line was either confined to the Pre-Function Area, or extended merely into the Rotunda. But this year, the line began in the hallway of the west wing of the main building (where the piano was), went down to the end of a short hallway section, made a right into another section of the hallway, made a left at the end of that section, made a right into the Coranado Ballroom at the end, went all the way around the room and out of the same door into the same hallway, followed the winding hallway again in reverse on the other side, went through the tower's main lobby, through the long Trinity Art Corridor, made a right into another long section of the corridor that led to the Rotunda in the Trinity building, made a left into a the same short hallway where we had to start waiting during previous QuakeCons, and finally into the Pre-Function Area where the registration desks were.

> Just before 11:20AM, after waiting in line for the first hour, I reached the Coranado Ballroom where I ate the second of four halves of a salami and turkey sandwich my mother had made me that morning. (I had the first half on the plane). Just before 3:30PM, I was in the Trinity Art Corridor that connected the main building with the Trinity building, where some volunteers handed out tiny black stickers to the attendees in line. The stickers read, "QuakeCon 2012 Equipment Tag", and had barcodes on them, and were meant to speed up the registration process once we got to the front of the line. I took two stickers, cut open both of my boxes, and put a sticker on the bottom of CES right under the one from the QuakeCon of 2010, and the other sticker on the top of the monitor. Just after 4PM, after waiting in line for a record five and a half hours, an older volunteer by the name of Ubermom handed out free QuakeCon 2012 cups to the people in line.

> Around 4:45PM, I started getting word from the other attendees that all of the First Come First Serve seats had been filled, and there were at least 200 people ahead of me in line. So I left CES and its monitor where they were on the floor and scouted ahead to investigate, and there were indeed at least 200 people. After getting back to my place in line, I began to see people come back with their computers in hand, and this supported the idea that the BYOC had in fact run out of FCFS seats. Around 7PM, one of the head volunteers by the name of Super Pumper announced that there were a number First Come First Serve seats still available, despite earlier concerns saying otherwise. He said that the volunteers were in the process of counting the empty seats, and that they were about to do a head count of the people in the front of the line and work their way back. When the number of people they counted was equal to the number of available seats, that would be the cutoff point, and the people in front of that point would get into the BYOC. The people behind that point would have to wait until tomorrow at 7PM, because that's when the $30 Select-A-Seat reserved packages would expire and be given to the people in the FCFS line.

> Soon after that announcement, the line moved forward by a small distance, perhaps only ten or fifteen feet. Some time after Super Pumper's announcement, the Quakeon staff began handing out free pizza from Pizza Hut to anyone in the line who would take some, and I took a plain slice for myself. The staff also handed out free cups of water, so I took one of those as well. A short time after that, an attendee who had seen the front of the line said, "This line is not fucking moving", and then said that somebody who was in the front of the line and had only six people ahead of him was turned away by the Quakecon staff because there were no more seats left.

> Convinced that I was going to be in my spot on the floor where I was standing for quite a while longer, I took CES and its monitor out of their boxes, set them up on the floor, and plugged them into the same outlet where I was charging my Droid before Super Pumper's announcement, but which was now behind me in the line. After hitting the power buttons for both CES and the monitor, the monitor wouldn't turn on, but I quickly solved that by pushing in the power plug which had come loose on the monitor's end. Still, nothing showed up on the monitor, and then I immediately looked inside CES through the top hatch windows and saw that the CPU fan wasn't spinning. As soon as I moved the stray wire that was blocking the fan, it started spinning again, but the boot process never completed. In fact, there was no sign of life from either the mouse or the USB hub, and the keyboard couldn't illuminate. Even after turning the system off and on again, then plugging out the power adapter and plugging it back in, the only things that turned on were the CPU fan, power LED, the four Y-cable LEDs, the four back fan LEDs, and the bottom fan grill LED. I didn't have time to investigate the problem any further, because at the point I had heard that the line was about to advance significantly, so I immediately unplugged CES and its monitor, and frantically packed everything back into their boxes and the backpack as fast as I could. The line started to move just as I was putting the last of the components away, and jumped another short distance which was probably fifteen or twenty feet.

> Sometime before 8:30PM, the volunteers addressed the line again, saying that they could make room for more seats by asking people already in the BYOC room to make their workstations smaller, essentially packing the attendees into tighter spaces. The number of attendees was also limited by power consumption issues, and only a certain number of devices could be supported. Sometime before 9:30PM, the line took a HUGE jump forward, and that was enough to take me out of the Trinity Art Corridor and into the Pre-Function Area of the Trinity building. Just after 10:30PM, a volunteer by the name of Bob approached me and told me that the QuakeCon staff had found a possible way to get us into the BYOC room. They offered the attendees who had been waiting line for the past 12 hours a choice; A: to stay in line and tough it out, or B: to get a voucher that would allow us to come back tomorrow after 6PM, and cut to the front of the line and get priority seating after the $30 Select-A-Seat reserved packages become available after 7PM. I chose to get the voucher, and when I did, I also got a free QuakeCon T-shirt. At this point, the line wasn't moving at all, and it seemed like the only way for it to advance now was for attendees already in the BYOC to leave with their equipment to make room for the people in line.

> Sometime after 11:30PM, I made an attempt to get CES to boot up properly. I took CES out of its box put it on a nearby table without the monitor, plugged in its power adapter and turned it on. Just like before, the machine's CPU heat sink fan, the power LED, and the LEDs of the Y-cables, back fan, and the bottom grill all turned on, but nothing else. I then unplugged the power adapter, and was about to reseat the RAM modules, and that's when I saw that the smaller one in the back was crooked and not parallel to the bigger one. So I lined up the smaller one to its socket, and pushed down on both sides to snap it in place. When I started the system then, the result was the same as before. Then, I plugged out CES, reaseated the big RAM stick, plugged CES back in, and turned on CES again. This time, the CD drive light blinked a few times about a second after the other LEDs turned on, and the HDD LED blinked a short time after that. This confirmed that CES was now working properly, and that this year's Quakecon trip had been saved. Just before midnight I brought CES and its monitor back to my hotel room, set up CES on the desk, charged my Droid, ate the third half of my mother's sandwich with some Coke, and spent the rest of the day starting the digital version of my QuakeCon log.
