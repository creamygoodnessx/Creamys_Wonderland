QuakeCon 2012 - Day Three

> I spent the opening hours of Saturday playing SuperTuxKart and trying unsuccessfully to get Quake 2 to run in OpenGL GLX mode. I had found a small group of BYOC attendees playing Quake 2, and I wanted to join in, but my inability to get the game to run on CES nixed that possibility. Around 4:15AM, I left the BYOC room to return to Room 574 to get some sleep, woke up around 8:45AM, took a shower, and returned to CES in the BYOC room.

> Around 12:15PM, I went up to the Modders Inc. booth in the vendor area in the Trinity Ballroom and asked about their case mod contest. The guy in the booth said that he could enter me in the contest if he knew the row and seat where my computer was located. When I checked the row number of my table, I found out that it was A50, so I relayed that info to the Modders Inc. person. He gave me a flyer with the number 22 written on it, and so I put that next to CES. Sometime before 1PM, some people from Modders Inc. checked out CES. They asked me a few questions about the machine, such as what inspired the machine's design, and the specs of the machine, and I answered those questions to the best of my ability. Around 1:45PM, I heard an announcement that the case mod contest would not be taking place at 2PM as originally scheduled, but rather at 7PM in the Chantilly Ballroom in the main building. At 5:30PM, an announcement came over the loudspeaker asking anyone taking part in the case mod contest to go immediately to the main ballroom. The numbers of the finalists were called, but unfortunately for me, 22 was not one of them, meaning that I failed yet again to get into the finals of a QuakeCon case mod contest.

> Later on that night, I was sitting at CES, and saw some QuakeCon attendees doing some work on an empty table right in front of me. When I asked what they were working on, an attendee by the name of Parker explained that his group was putting together a machine that was assembled entirely from parts donated from other attendees from around the BYOC. The machine had no chassis, and was consisted of an Intel i7 processor, 8GB of RAM, and two GTX570 video cards. The group's goal was to build a powerful computer from scratch, and have Parker wear it on his back while wearing a monitor on his chest. During our conversation, Parker's team had finished the build, and tested the machine by turning it on. The machine started up without any apparent problems, and there was a bad-ass looking illuminated blue logo on the motherboard. Soon after the successful bench test, Parker's team used various cables and duct tape to fix a power supply to his left leg, the chassis to his back, and the monitor to his chest. Once the rig was secured to Parker, he and his team tested the machine again, and then exited the BYOC room to hang out in the outdoor smoking area just outside the Rotunda for a while. When they came back to the BYOC room, they returned to the table in front of CES and played around with WordPad for a while, where funny messages were printed out on Parker's screen with giant fonts. Soon afterwards, Parker took off the rig.

> Throughout that same day, I kept myself occupied by sitting at CES and playing more SuperTuxKart, and a little bit of Stepmania and MUGEN. I also made a few more attempts to get Quake 2 running in OpenGL GLX mode, but try as I might, I just couldn't do it. When I wasn't sitting at CES, I was walking around the BYOC room checking out the case mods, and exploring the Trinity Ballroom where I bought a Doom T-shirt for $18, and a Notepal X3 laptop cooler with blue LEDs for $30. As for food, the Pizza Hut truck had returned that day, and I got a large pepperoni pizza for $15, and a 20 ounce Pepsi for $2, and that was my meal for Saturday.
