QuakeCon 2003 - Day Two

> I continued to sit at Glamdring through midnight, deciding what to do as I ran Ad-Aware out of sheer boredom. I figured that since my first day couldn't possibly get any worse, logic suggested that the second day had to be better, so I chose to give my vacation a chance to somehow redeem itself. I went into the lobby to try to order a pizza, but all of the pizza place in the downtown Dallas area were either closed or didn't deliver that late at night. Disappointed yet again, I staggered back to the BYOC room at around 1AM and noticed a large crowd gathered around the left side of the NOC. They were watching these two guys playing a game called Stepmania, which was projected on the wall. The game wasn't played with a standard gamepad, a keyboard, or a joystick. Instead, the players danced on these large pads, each was a 3x3 grid with an left, up, down, and right arrow, each on it appropriate square on the grid. The screen was split down the middle, and each player controlled a side. Each side had four transparent arrows on the top of the screen, left, up, down and right, lined up horizontally. From the bottom of the screen, flashing arrows would rise up at varying speeds, and meet the transparent ones at the top of the screen. The goal for each player was to wait until a flashing arrow met its transparent counterpart, then step on the appropriate arrow on the pad. Often, the players needed to step on two arrows at once. There was dance music in the background, and the correct dance pattern corresponded with the music. (The players could pick from nearly 900 songs.) The background wasn't simply a solid color, but rather a variety of animated wallpapers, designed not only to look cool, but to be a potential distracting agent. Stepmania was a truly awesome spectator sport, because it was just so damn cool to watch two people dancing in unison to Japanese dance music, as well as a few American dance classics, especially when the music got really fast, and the players had trouble keeping up. Priceless, made me forget about the crappy time I was having so far.

> I went back to Glamdring, and tinkered with the network settings a little more, but still couldn't connect to QuakeCon's network. Then, on a whim, I plugged my Ethernet cable to another port on the LinkSys box, something I tried once before; this would be the third port I would try. Right after I did that, seemingly miraculously, I got an Internet connection! So I thought, OK, I've got Web access, so maybe my vacation won't ENTIRELY suck after all. It wasn't much of a connection, only 10Kb per second at best, but dammit it was a connection nevertheless. However, I still had trouble connecting to Quake 3 hosts on the network, so I thought that now that I had Web access, I could download the latest Q3A patch, and hope that would fix the problem. I spent the next several hours online, attempting to download the the latest Quake 3 patch (v1.32). but I kept disconnecting. I then disabled my screen saver, downloaded from a different server, and let it sit. It was now 5AM, and I'd been without sleep or anything good to eat for at least 24 hours. Since I had nothing important to do, and there were less than 5 people playing Stepmania, I thought to myself, "What the hell, I'll try it out." It was an interesting experience, but not really of my genre of choice.

> I then felt a sudden urge to have some of my Twizzlers, so I headed up to my room. When I got there, I reached into my left pocket to grab my wallet, only to discover that the bulge in my pocket I'd been feeling for the past few hours wasn't my wallet, but just a really big wad of one dollar bills I got from the guy who bought my adapter. So I went to the front desk for help, and the lady behind the counter told me to go upstairs and wait for the security guard to arrive. After about 10 minutes, the guard showed up and opened my door. I then showed him my ID, and everything was cool.

> When I returned to Glamdring, the patch download was in the 80% range, and didn't take much longer to finish. After installing the patch, I tried Quake 3 again by connecting to a FFA server, and was expecting to get the same error message I'd been getting (forgot what it was.) To my surprise, Glamdring started to download a 35MB file from the server. Just then, I noticed a crowd gathered around the large projection screen to the right of the noc, so once again letting Glamdring sit for a download, I went up to get a closer look. Someone was playing short and amusing movie clips, most of which I already had in my movie archives. One clip showed a pizza delivery guy getting hit by a car, and another was a British salmon ad where a fisherman was fighting against a bear. There were also a few clips of sporting accidents.

> When I returned to Glamdring again, the 35MB was only 5% finished, just where it was when I left to watch the movie clips, so I was like "Ah to hell with it.", and disconnected from the server. After giving a few more servers a try, I was eventually playing my first Q3A LAN game of QuakeCon 2003! At last, it felt that I was starting to gain back some lost ground on my trip, and that it wouldn't suck so bad after all. I thought to myself, "OK, so maybe I didn't get to play in a tournament this year. But hey, I'm in the BYOC, and that's what I originally came for, so I'm not going to complain."

> Sometime around dawn, I noticed someone on his PC just in front of the noc playing Postal 2. I went to get a closer look at the game, and soon and small crowd followed. It was a first person shooter about an antisocial, homicidal madman who kills innocent people. There are a variety of weapons, most of them unconventional, and meant to make their victims suffer before dying, rather than "one shot, one kill" weapons. For example, you can chase down people with a can of gasoline, throw a match on them, and watch them run around and burn. If you shock someone with a stun gun, that person will drop to the floor, curl up in a fetal position, and then start pissing uncontrollably. Even a stream of piss can be used as a weapon. It's slightly green and apparently highly toxic, because if you keep the stream on someone long enough, that person will vomit blood. However, if you go to far with the killing spree, the police and some armed civilians can fight back and kill you. However, the player I was watching didn't seem to take any damage, because it looked like he was using some kind of a cheat code. Postal 2 is a fun game to watch for a while, but I wouldn't buy it, because I have a feeling it has very little replay value.

> I finally broke my 30+ hour "fast" just before 9 that morning. (If you ignore the french fries I had at the airport just before my flight the previous day.) I went downstairs to the first floor, and gave the hotel's dining area a try. My breakfast was nothing special, just a buffet consisting of coffee, bread and butter, a small bowl of Fruit Loops, and some milk.

> I had been awake for 27 and a half hours straight when I got back to the BYOC after breakfast, but for some reason, I just didn't feel tired. After playing a few more rounds of Quake, I unexpectedly got a chance to redeem myself for missing my slot in the Quake 3 tourney. At around 1PM, an announcement came over the loudspeaker that the Generations Arena mod team was holding a contest, and I immediately jumped at the chance to join. The Generations people gave all the contests installation disks for the mod, and I installed my copy right away. I sure as hell wasn't going to miss my turn at this tourney too, so I asked someone at the Generations mod team when the contest was going to start, and he said it was going to be around 5 or so.

> Since it wasn't even 2PM yet, I figured that I had enough time to get myself better prepared for the Generations tournament. I was going to use my no-frills keyboard, but since I had sold my PS/2 to USB adapter the previous day, that was no longer an option, so I planned on buying another such adapter, or a USB keyboard. So I went to the hotel's front desk, and asked the lady behind the desk if there was a Radio Shack nearby. She said there was one in a mall one train stop north of the hotel. I was a bit nervous about taking the train by myself, but I got there without too much of a hassle. (I overshot my destination by one stop, but took the southbound train and corrected my mistake.) Radio Shack didn't have anything I could use, but OfficeMax did; a USB keyboard from Logitech.

> I got back to the hotel at around 4:15PM, just enough time to order a pizza and have a few slices. I ordered a pepperoni pizza and a 20oz Coke from Pizza Hut, and I was told my order would arrive in about 45 minutes. I used this time to get in some Generations practice, and to get used to my new keyboard. At 5:30, I went to check if the pizza guy had arrived yet, and went to the front desk to check if he had. The people behind the desk said there was someone here from the pizza place, and he had been waiting for me for a while. So I rushed towards the elevators to get upstairs to my sixth floor room, when I just happened to pass the pizza guy in the lobby, heading out of the hotel. He said he'd been waiting by my door for the past ten minutes before he finally gave up and decided to leave. So I tipped him an extra dollar to make up for the inconvenience.

> I settled down in an eating area in the lobby about 15 feet away from where I met the pizza guy, when I noticed a guy on a couch about 40 feet in front and to the left of me. I could only see his back, but I saw what looked to be the back of a Polycount T-shirt, which was what I was wearing at the time. I took a closer look, and sure enough, it was a Polycount T-shirt. When I got closer still, I realized that its wearer was none other than Drew "rogue13" Risch (Polycount's admin) with a few of his friends. I talked to him for a minute or two, and he said that although he wasn't originally able to make it to QuakeCon, he got a lucky break at the last minute. After talking to r13, I went back to where I was sitting, ate a couple slices of pizza, and brought the rest of it to my room. There was no fridge, so I was forced to leave it out on a table.

> I got back to Glamdring just before 5 that evening, and found out the rules of the first round of the Generations tourney. There were to be four free-for-all matches, with eight players in each match, and only the top four out of those eight would advance to the next round. Since I wasn't in the first match, and the second one wasn't scheduled for another 45 minutes, I decided to get a little shut-eye. So I cleared a spot on my table, put my jacket on the table in a rolled up position, then rested my head on the jacket. After 35 continuous hours of no sleep, the little rest that I got then was quite a relief. I couldn't take a picture of myself sleeping, but if I could, I would have looked something like this guy.

> When the second match of the tournament happened around 5:45, I found that I wasn't in that match either, so I went back to sleep, though I don't know how long I was asleep for. When I did wake up, I went back to the Generations table to make sure I didn't miss my turn, which I mercifully didn't. On my way back to my table, I noticed someone playing a remake of Doom called JDoom, which basically looked like Doom with OpenGL features. The guy was playing the Doom 2 level Tricks & Traps, and it was hilarious watching him swear a lot as he kept getting killed. When my turn finally came in the fourth match of the first round at 10PM, I showed up on the server along with two other guys. Since the other five guys never showed up, the three of us won by default. The rest of the night is kind of a blur to me, but I do remember playing more Quake 3 on the network, taking a picture of my new table setup, and trying unsuccessfully to get KaZaA Lite to work right.
