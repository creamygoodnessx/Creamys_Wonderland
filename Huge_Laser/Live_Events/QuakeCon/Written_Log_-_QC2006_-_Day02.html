QuakeCon 2006 - Day Two

> I started out the second day of the QuakeCon listening to mp3 and surfing the Web on CES, as well as playing Street Fighter Zero 3 on Game Not Over, a modded PC that was built to look like a life size arcade machine, and ran a MAME emulator. It had controls to support up to four players, but only the first two sets of controls were functional. The machine ran about a hundred different games, including Dragon's Lair, Guitar Hero, and Windjammers. Just before 3:30AM, some people on the Noc, the stage in the center of the BYOC, announced that they were giving away free T-shirts, and soon several people rush in and surrounded the Noc. Several people on the Noc, including Nash, began tossing T-shirts into the crowd at random. They then pointed to each of the Noc's four sides and asked the crowd on each side to scream, and then they threw several shirts to the loudest side. I was among the people who tried to grab a shirt, but unfortunately I struck out. Later on, I took my first shower of the event, and then went back to bed around 6AM, and got managed to get another three and a half hours of sleep.

> At 11AM, I took part in my first competition of the QuakeCon. It was a photo scavenger hunt, and each contestant was given a list of things to take pictures of. The rules were to photograph as many items on the list, and we only had one hour to take the pictures, and burn them to a CD. The items included booths in the BYOC room, decorations in the hotel's main building, attendees wearing T-shirts of previous QuakeCons, and a variety of other things. Around 11:45PM, after taking several pictures, I realized that I had no way to burn them to a CD, so I was forced to withdraw from the contest.





> Sometime after 1PM, the QuakeCon staff was tossing more T-shirts into a crowd of eager attendees. This time, it was being done from the nVidia stage instead of the Noc, and the shirts were being tossed by some guy in a black Doom marine-like costume on stilts. I was somewhere in the back of the crowd in another attempt to grab myself a free shirt, and even though I came close to grabbing two shirts, I was unsuccessful again. I did however manage to take some footage of the T-shirt toss on my Sony Cybershot digital camera.

> Besides the nVidia stage, there was also an nVidia booth on the other side of the BYOC room that was giving away free stuff of its own, which were primarily Enemy Territory T-shirts and lighted disks. These domed, plastic disks had paper backs, and when the paper side was crushed in, the disk would give off this bright green glow, and then fade gradually over the course of a few hours. I took a shirt and a disc, and put the disc inside CES's chassis on top of the CD drive to give CES a temporary extra glow. Other people also used the discs to enhance their case mods as well, and some were even wearing theirs on their clothes, or even as the clothing itself.

> At 2PM, a Guitar Hero tournament began at the nVidia stage. Guitar Hero is a music oriented game for the Playstation 2, in which two players follow rhythmic patterns on the screen on guitar-like controllers while music plays in the background. The songs included several songs I grew up listening to, including Killer Queen by Queen, and Iron Man by Black Sabbath. Since I had never played the game before, I decided not to play myself, but to just enjoy it as a spectator. I watched this tournament on and off throughout the next two days, taking several pictures and shooting a few movies.



> Later on that night, I saw that even more free stuff was being tossed into a crowd, this time from a stage close to the Activision section where the official Quake 4 and Enemy Territory tournaments were being played. This time around, mousepads and orange balls were being given away instead of T-shirts. One of the mousepads landed in an empty booth that was immediately to my left, so I climbed over the barrier into the booth to grab it, but someone beat me to it by about one second. Luckily, a second pad landed in the booth, and I managed to snag this one. I went over to CES, put my tiny blue mousepad in the ice maker box, and replaced it with the new Patriot Memory pad I had just gotten.

> I spent the next few hours playing TMNT: Turtles In Time on Game Not Over, and watching some more Guitar Hero. I also downloaded a few replacement icons for some of CES's folders, and downloaded iNES, an 8-bit NES emulator that ran on Linux. The ROMs I got with the emulator included Double Dragon 2, Bad Dudes, and Vice: Project Doom, and while the games ran a bit fast and choppy, I still had some fun with them. Three tables in front of me, there was a group of players from tastyspleen, an online community that runs Quake 2 servers. One of the players was running a version of the game called EGL Quake 2, which ran the game with super-enhanced graphics, making some of the objects glow much more brightly. I tried to join the game the tastyspleen guys were playing, but even through I was given the IP address of their server, I still couldn't connect to it on CES.
