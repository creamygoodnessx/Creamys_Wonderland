1/12/2004 - In the pre-dawn hours of this morning, I was studying my front panel, and trying to figure out exactly how to mount the switches and LED shades. I found that the best way to do this is to mount the Schweppes bottle top first on the middle left edge. Once that is done, I can figure out exactly where to cut the hole for the reset button, which will be just above bottle top. While playing around with my front panel components, I found that the red cap_fit so neatly over the reset button, it could be used to easily mark the location of the reset button hole. The button won't stick very far out of the panel, but I think that cutting the hole at an inward angle on each of the four sides might give a finger more room to push the button. I plan to see if I can do this without ruining the panel by doing a test on the top half of the cart piece. The rectangular holes that were already on the back of the cart piece are very close in size to the future reset button hole. After cutting off the protrusions that are on either side of one of the holes, I will poke the reset button into it from the back, and see how well it sticks through the front. I'll then cut around the four sides hole at an inward angle to see if I can make the hole bigger without ruining the plastic around it. If I can do this, I will cut the hole for the reset button in the same manner. Finally, I will cut the two holes for the LED shades below the Schweppes bottle top, making sure the bottle top is squarely in between the two. After I cut the clear lipstick cap down to the size of the red cap, both will then be glued into place. Since the locations of the buttons and switches are going to revolve around the Schweppes bottle top, I'll have to wait until I finish drinking an entire 2 liter bottle before using its top. I'm about two thirds through one of the bottles now, so hopefully I can finish it soon.

1/10/2004 - A little after 2:30 this morning, I resumed work on my front panel's drive holes. After I finished chiseling dents around the inner parimeter of the slimline CD drive bay, I completed the holes by chiseling them through the back of the panel. After a little confusion on how to continue, I began to work on the drive hole's corners, which proved to be a bit more difficult to complete. It was basically more chiseling and cutting, just a lot more intense than the initial holes. Next, I had to deal with the protruding sections on the back of the panel, which intercepted my parimeter of holes in six places. But like the circle I had to deal with while cutting out the panel itself, they weren't all that hard to chisel through. The rest was just a matter of playing connect-the-dots with the parimeter holes with the box cutter, then using the thin screwdriver and rubber mallet to chisel out the rest of drive bay hole. I soon found that the drive bay hole was not only a little crooked, but also a bit too small for my broken ThinkPad CD drive. I've already made the bay hole a little less crooked through a bit of chiseling and planing the edges, but I still have to make the hole big enough for the drive to fit. It's 7:45AM, and I'm too tired to work anymore for now, so I'll get myself a little shut-eye before continuing any further.

> This afternoon, I continued to fine tune my front panel's CD drive bay. The hole was too short and too narrow for my ThinkPad CD drive to fit through, so I had to make it bigger. So I used the box cutter to gradually plane the bottom of the hole bigger wider. I soon realized this wasn't going to be practical, so using my favorite ruler, I drew a new cut line about a millimeter below the hole. I then used the box cutter widen the hole. I also had to make the hole longer by cutting several millimeters out of it's left side. I accidentally made another crack in my panel, but a little bit of Krazy Glue took care of that. I kept gradually making the hole bigger until the CD drive could fit through. This was another all day job, and I've got the mess to prove it.

> I've made a slight change to my plans for my drive bays. Originally, I was going to mount the floppy drive just below the CD drive, without leaving any space between them. But now, I think I'll separate them by about two or three millimeters of space. Speaking of drives, I haven't yet figured out how to mount the floppy drive and the hard drive to my skeleton. Until I know for sure, I'm going to refrain from cutting the floppy drive bay hole in my front panel. I'm either going to use some of my 3.5" mounts, or just buy a two-drive mount online.

1/9/2004 - Yesterday afternoon, I took another run through my mother's dresser drawers in search of a clear shade for my power LED in order to take the place of my Pepsi bottle cap. I managed to find another unused lipstick cap, but this one was completely transparent, as opposed to the lipstick cap that's now a part of my custom made power button. At first, I was going to use an unknown plug-like object as the shade, but I feel the LED will shine better through the clear lipstick cap. All I have to do is hacksaw down the cap to the size of my red HDD shade and it will be ready to glue to my front panel.

> Late last night and through midnight this morning, I was about to save my Veo's photos to my project's Original photos directory on Candybox, and was viewing the directory's images with Paint Shop Pro 4, when I noticed that there weren't as many pictures as there should have been. So as a measure of caution, I created a sub-directory and copied the files to it. Then, I downloaded the photos from the camera to the Original directory. My fears were confirmed as I watched file after file get overwritten, because my files were named 'pic01', 'pic02', etc., and the camera was naming the new files 'pic##' starting at 01, rather than starting one pic higher than my last 'pic##'. If I hadn't noticed the problem, I would have permanently lost the pics I took of me cutting down the front panel, and those are pics I obviously can't retake. This wasn't always a problem, because I used to save the files onto a special album on Glamdring, a method which managed the filenames and prevented unwanted overwrites. From that special directory, I would then copy the files onto Original on Candybox. But since I started copying the files directly onto Candybox after my dumpster diving expedition, I've been unknowingly overwriting files and losing my photo log. However, the problem isn't as bad as you might think. In addition to saving my recorded progress on the sawing of the front panel, I also remembered that I put a lot of this month's pics into a zip file. When I took a look at those pics, I found that all the pics from my dumpster diving adventure to some I took of my then unopened LED pack were safe. The only pics I really lost were the ones where I was testing the new LEDs. So from now on, every time I download pics from the camera onto Candybox, they will be saved to a new sub-directory.

> It's 11:30PM, and I'm still working on cutting out the drive bays from CES's front panel. However, before I could start cutting, I used a small white GTFM ruler to verify that the cut lines for the drive bays were straight. They were uneven, so I had to redraw them before continuing. Because the drive holes were in an enclosed space, using the hacksaws was out of the question. So used the box cutter and rubber mallet to perform the chisel method to cut out the drives, starting with the slimline CD drive bay. Playing it safe, I chiseled along the inner edge of the cut lines. However, I soon developed a problem. When I reached the right edge of the panel, a small crack began to form on the edge of the panel. So I put an eraser under the crack and continued to chisel, and the crack didn't get any bigger as I worked. I plan to put some Krazy Glue in the crack to fix it, and I'll possibly even attach a thin metal plate over it to further ensure it doesn't cause me any more problems. I will continue to work on the panel past midnight, so I'll have to continue updating my progress tomorrow.

1/8/2004 - When I was down in the basement pantry last night, I was putting away some Pepsi bottles I'd bought that day, when I noticed that some Schweppes bottles on the floor. These bottles were green and had white caps, a perfect color combination for my power button front panel sheath. When I finish the 2 liter Pepsi that's currently in the fridge, my next soda will be the Schweppes. When I'm done with that, I will hack off the top of the bottle, and use it as my new power button sheath, replacing the Pepsi bottle top.

> Today, I was hard at work on chopping down my shopping cart piece, and let me tell you, it wasn't nearly as quick and easy as I thought it would be. At around 6:30PM, I used a hacksaw to cut a small notch on either side of the cart piece 4 inches from the bottom. In order to make the sawing easier, I put the blade end of a box cutter on the outer edge of the cut line just beyond the notch I just cut. I then used a rubber mallet and pounded the back end of the box cutter, etching a small dent on the line. I made a few more dents along the cut line with this method, and the dents were about a quarter to half an inch apart. I then used a thin screwdriver and the mallet to deepen the dents in the chiseling manner as with the box cutter. When I did this, the dents were deep enough to be seen from the back of the panel. Interchanging between two hacksaws, I sawed down as far down the cut line as I could, and the dents were really making the job easier. But that's when I hit a snag. After sawing down about a third of the way down, the back of the hacksaw hit the edge of the panel, preventing me from sawing any further down. I repeated the chiseling and hacksawing a third of the way down on the other side again, but after that, I was at a loss about how to continue. Not entirely sure that I knew what I was doing, I used the box cutter and screwdriver chiseling method for the middle third of the line, then made a slash along the line with the box cutter. Because the new middle dents were visible from the back of the panel, I used the dents as a guide and I slashed across them. The final obstacle was the protruding circle at the back of the panel, which was the toughest part to cut through. It was fairly easy for me to use the screwdriver to chisel through the circle. Once the circle was out of the way, I only needed a few more slashes to bend the cart piece at the cut line. After taking a couple of snapshots of this bending, I finally broke the cart piece into two pieces. As a final step, I sanded down the edge to get rid of the fraying and to make it smoother. The whole process was far more of a bitch than I could have ever imagined; it took me about four and a half hours to get the job done. I'm not going to bother cleaning up the mess I made getting my front panel to this point, because tomorrow, I will cut out the drive bay holes.

1/7/2004 - I went to Jen and JD's house to babysit the kids for a little while, and brought my LED pack, Pepsi bottle cap, checker, a Duracel AA battery, a red and yellow cable, my Veo, and some red, caplike piece of plastic that came with a floor lamp I got for Christmas. Using my fingers, I held each of the exposed wires of one end of the cable to each terminal of the battery, and touched the wires on the other end of the cable to one of my small green LEDs. Nothing happened, and I couldn't figure out why. When JD got back home, he helped me light the LED. He stripped one end off of the cable with my big sapphire Swiss Army Knife. He then took the two AAs out of the flashlight on my keychain, and had me hold the positive end of one battery to the negative end of the other, while holding the end of the red wire to the positive end of the front battery, and the yellow wire to the negative end of the back battery. While I was doing that, JD touched the LED to the now exposed wires on the other end of the cable. The light lit for the first time, and when we tried shining it through the Pepsi cap, it appeared dimly from under the cap. When we lit up the LED that appeared to be white, it turned out to really give off a red glow.

> After I got home, I decided to experiment further with the LEDs. I used electrical tape to attach one end of the cable to two batteries in the same manner I did at JD's place. I then wrapped the wires on the other end of the cable to various LEDs. In order to see the glow of the LED's more clearly, I turned off the lights in the kitchen. From my experiments with the LEDs, I found that the white-sheathed red LEDs were brighter than the red-sheathed red LEDs. I also discovered that none of the LEDs shined brightly enough through the Pepsi cap for my tastes. So here's what I now plan to do for my front panel. I will use the white-sheathed red LED as my HDD light, and cover it with the red plastic cap that came with my floor lamp. For my power light, I will use a green LED, and use a clear shade, possibly an unknown plug-like object I found weeks ago. There were no blue LEDs in the pack, so that's why I'm using a green one now. When I have the time, I'll change the cut lines in my front panel to accommodate the new covers. (It's a damn good thing I didn't rush to cut out the holes for the Pepsi bottle top and the checker. :)) I'll also need to adapt the green and red LEDs to fit my motherboard. The change in lampshades turns out to be surprisingly convenient, because the new shades will be smaller than the Pepsi cap and checker, meaning I no longer have to worry about weather or not they'll fit on my front panel.

1/4/2004 - At around 2:00 this morning, I started marking the cut lines on the front piece of a shopping cart as the first step of converting it into the front panel of CES. The first mark I made was a horizontal line all the way across the panel, exactly four inches off the bottom. Next, I used my Pepsi bottle top and a checker as guides to mark two circles in the bottom left corner where the power button and power light will go. I was going to make a small rectangle where the reset button was going to stick through the panel, but I couldn't determine its exact location. I also decided to leave out the hole for the HDD light for the time being. The last lines I drew where the ones for the CD drive and the floppy drive; I used the broken laptop CD drive from my IBM_ThinkPad_Glamdring and my broken floppy drive as guides. My next step now is to cut the cart piece at the 4" line, getting a better feel of the true size of the panel. Next, I'm going to cut out the Floppy and CD drive bays, making sure my broken drives will fit neatly into them. However, I'm going to leave out the power and reset buttons for now. I have two reasons for doing this. First, the power button and power light will both use a Pepsi bottle top. Since I currently have only one such top, I had to use the checker as a substitute, because it's close in size to the bottle top. I'm not sure if it's a close enough substitute, so I'm going to decapitate another Pepsi bottle before cutting the holes in my panel. Second, I have yet to test an actual LED light to see if it will shine brightly enough through the Pepsi cap and checker for me to use them as my lampshades. If they're not, I'll have to either find brighter lights, or a thinner shades.

After buying batteries for my Veo, some Hot Tamales, and having a pizza lunch, I went to the back of the strip mall and looked in some various dumpsters for a potential front panel for my Creamy Entertainment System. I was looking for a thin but sturdy piece of sheet metal or plastic that was at least 12" x 4". One dumpster had some pink plastic container, which I think was too short for me to use. Even if I could, it was too far down for me too reach without stepping into the dumpster. Another dumpster had some black plastic electronic device I couldn't recognize, but again, it was too far down reach without messing myself up. A search of the remaining dumpsters yielded no results. I noticed some black plastic box lying on the grass just beyond the pavement, but a closer examination made me think twice about touching it. 12/31/2003 - It's 14 minutes before the new year, and I've just finished drawing a simulation of the side panels of CES. I took a blank piece of printing paper, then drew a 4" x 7" rectangle on it with the help of my favorite ruler. I then drew dots down and across the edges of the rectangle, five millimeters apart, and used those dots to draw a 36 x 16 square grid. The vertical lines were drawn first, because it was more tedious to draw 36 lines than to draw 16, and I wanted to get the hard part out of the way quickly. Once the grid was completed, I filled in the grid with the appropriate colors with colored pencils. Jen arrived late in the night to accompany me for the new years, and she brought over some colored pencils for me to borrow. The sim panel looks good, and I think I'll go with this method with the real thing, but with permanent markers. Instead of putting the name of the computer on the bottom of the side panel like I was planning before, I will now put it on the top above the flames. After the grid is completely filled in, I plan to use small Sharpie markers to carefully draw the letters on, in the LOZ: A Link to the Past_font. The letters will be a quarter inch wide and a half inch tall, and the words will no longer have to be unbroken.
