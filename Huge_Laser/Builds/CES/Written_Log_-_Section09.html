CES Report - Section 9:
 5/12/2004 to 6/15/2004

6/15/2004 - It's almost 3:30 in the afternoon, and I'm just about to start putting together CES's left top hatch door. I'm going to build this door a little differently from how I built the right door. This time, I'm going to leave the protective tape on the aluminum panel, and the packaging paper on the lexan panel, in order to reduce unwanted scratching. The last time I worked on the top hatch, I had made marks for the drill holes on the underside of the left door, where light-colored tape was used. I plan to drill the holes in the aluminum panel without the corresponding lexan panel. Once the holes are drilled, I will place the aluminum panel with the black tape facing upwards, on top of the lexan panel. I'll then drill through the drilled holes in the aluminum panel, and into the lexan.

> It's just after 9:30PM right now, and I've finished drilling the holes in the top hatch's left aluminum panel, but things didn't go too smoothly. The corner bolt hole for the roller catch was WAY too high despite my best effort to keep it low. I had to do so much correcting that the hole ended up with an oblong shape. I also had do to some corrections to the hinge bolt holes, but they weren't as severe as the corner roller catch hole. I used a different method for drilling the hinge bolt holes for this door than I did for the right door. Instead of drilling each hole while periodically checking them by putting the corresponding hinges over them, I drilled a hole at one of the ends, and then bolted the appropriate hinge hole into place. I then put the hinge in the right position by pressing the edge of the hinge evenly against the edge of the panel, and this told me where the hinge's other two bolt holes should go if the hinge was to go on straight. I then drilled the holes with progressively larger drill bits until I had holes that were just the right size to fit the bolts in place. One thing I noticed as I worked was that the drill chewed through the aluminum a lot more easily than it chewed through the brass hinges. In fact, the only damage the drill did to the hinges was peel back a sharp section, so I may have to file it down someday. All I have to do now is make the matching holes in the lexan panel, then clean debris from the panels, remove the protective tape/paper, and then clean up the mess on the kitchen table.

6/10/2004 - Yesterday evening, I continued to work on my top hatch doors. I was originally going to do this some weekend at JD's house, but I've come to realize that I don't really need his fancy high end equipment to get the job done. After laying out the protective newspaper on the table, I used my X-acto knife to saw away the small bits of metal that still held the middle of one of my hatch doors in place. If you recall from an earlier log entry, I used a Dremel to cut the perimeter of the windows in the aluminum, but didn't saw out the corners in fear of damaging the outer part of my panels. When I began sawing the corner sections of the panel, I didn't know if the X-acto knife was going to be able to complete the perimeter cut, but slowly the corner grooves met, and the middle section began to separate from the outer section. Some parts were tough to saw through, so I used a thin, flat screwdriver and a rubber mallet to chisel them out, a similar method I used to build my front panel. Eventually, I was able to sever all of the bits of aluminum connecting the panel's inner section to its outer section, and remove the inner section from the panel. After I cut out the middle sections of both aluminum top hatch panels, I used my mother's postal scale to weigh the sections to see how much weight was coming off of my computer. The sections weighed three and a half ounces, which was exactly what I'd predicted nearly a month ago.

> After taking the Ives_middle hinges and one of the roller catches out of their packages, I used a Penstix black marker to mark the drill holes on my aluminum hatch doors. The brass knobs were to go in the middle of the inner edges of the doors, while the knob ends of the roller catches were to be mounted vertically above the knobs, on the underside of the doors. The hinges were to go on the underside of the outer edges of the hatch doors, so I marked that area with six drill hole marks. When I made the marks, I made sure to have the less damaged sides of the panels facing up.

> I opened up a package of 8/32" angle head slotted screws and fitted one into a hole in one of my hinges. The angled part of the screw didn't seem to fit as snugly as I hoped it would, so I put one of the hinge's wood screws in another hole for comparison. The wood screw fit in perfectly, but I decided to use the ones from the separate package, since they were made for machining, rather than wood. So I then opened up one of the packages of brass nuts, and used one to secure the machine screw to the hinge. The result was a solid fit, so I figured that I had nothing to worry about.

> When I was ready to drill, I put one of my lexan panels under what would be the right aluminum top hatch panel, and used four pieces of clear Scotch tape to hold them together. I then put the panels on top of two of my angle irons so the drill wouldn't go through the cutting board I was using to protect the table. I then hammered a small nail into the top right hole for the roller catch. This was to create a pilot hole for the drill. I then fitted the smallest drill bit (a 3/32" bit) I had to my dad's cordless Panasonic drill, and then drilled the hole through both panels. The hole wasn't big enough for the 3/8" zinc bolt to fit through, so I widened the hole with a 7/64" bit. The bolt fit into the hole perfectly, and so I repeated the process with the roller catch's other hole. After I made the hole for the brass knob, I bolted the knob and the roller catch's knob into their places. It was then when I realized I made a crucial error. The roller catch knob was mounted vertically, but the roller could only be mounted to the 12" top angle iron horizontally. So I drilled another hole for the roller catch to the right of the catch's top drill hole. It was then a simple matter of turning the catch's knob 90 degrees clockwise so that it would use the top two drill holes, and be mounted horizontally. I decided it was better to leave the bottom bolt in place than it was to have an empty hole. In order to preserve the top hatch's symmetry, I will make a matching bottom roller catch bolt hole on the left hatch door as well.

> As I worked, I noticed that the drill hole marks I drew for the hinges were rubbing off on my hands. After I got tired redrawing the marks with the Penstix, I switched to using my new pink mechanical pencil instead. It was after midnight when I started drilling the holes for the hinges. By this time, I had removed the Scotch tape that was holding my panels together, because the existing bolts were enough to keep the panels from slipping apart. As I drilled the holes for the top hinge, a slip of the drill caused me to miss the mark on the bottom hole. I tried to correct the problem by re-adjusting the hole as I drilled downwards, but the drill kept slipping back into the wrong position. I found that if I drilled at an angle towards the correct position, I could get the hole to end up closer to where it needed to be. At around two in the morning, I finished drilling the three holes, using drill bits as large as 5/32". I then bolted one of the middle hinges in place, using three brass nuts to secure the hinge on the topside.

> It's just after three in the afternoon right now, and I spent all day typing out this report. I will continue to work on the hatch doors today and then record my progress in the following paragraphs. But before I begin, I'd like to point out that I just took another look at the hinge I bolted on this morning, and seems a little crooked. If it becomes a problem, I'm not sure how I'll fix it and still make it look good, but I'll cross that bridge when I get to it.

> It's now after 11PM, and I've made some good progress on my top hatch today. I planned to bolt one of the hinges that JD gave me on the outer bottom corner of the panel, right next to the hinge I bolted on yesterday. As I tried to drill the first hole for the bottom hinge, the drill bit kept rolling away from where I was trying to put the hole. After a few more tries, I drilled the first hole with the smallest drill bit, but the hole was too low. However, I was able to use the bigger bits to adjust the hole to it's correct position. The second hole was also severely off when I first drilled it, but I managed to correct its position too. After all three holes were drilled, I bolted JD's hinge to the holes, and it was a perfect fit. Just to make sure the hinges weren't crooked, I pressed them against the side of the kitchen table, and they turned out to be straight enough.

> My next step was to clean off the newly completed hatch door of all of the debris that was trapped between the panels. I unbolted everything from the hatch door and separated the two panels. I then cleaned the lexan sheet by first wiping it down with a paper towel, then rinsing it off with water, and finally soaping it it up with a sponge and rinsing it off again. As for the aluminum panel, I removed the tape that was still left on it, then erased the remaining pencil lines. However, doing so left marks on the panel I couldn't remove, even with soap. I re-assembled the hatch door, but it was too late to start working on the left door, so I'll have to take care of that after I get back from some wedding in Canada I've been invited to.

5/23/2004 - Yesterday, I was thinking about how I was going to fit the CES's name on one of its side panels. I had a 10 millimeter square of space to paint each letter, but the letters were 7 x 7 pixels. This meant that I needed to evenly break down each square into a 7 x 7 grid, but none of my ruler's measurements could do this. I could have divided each square into a 10 x 10 grid, but this would have meant having 7 square millimeters of letter, and then three millimeters of space before the next letter. This was more empty space than I was comfortable with having, plus the letters would have been too small for my tastes. After much thought, I came up with a solution. If I put a one pixel border around each letter, I could increase the size of each letter to nine square millimeters, and have only a one millimeter space separating each letter. While some of the authentic feel of the NES font would be lost, the letters would now look so much cooler in a two-color state.

> In order to see what the name would look like once painted onto my side panel, I made a simulation of the name as a GIF image. Using screenshots from Super Mario Brothers and The Legend of Zelda, I wrote the words "THE CREAMY ENTERTAINMENT SYSTEM" in three rows in the classic NES font. I drew the letters bright green and surrounded each letter with a dark green border, using a black background. I spaced each letter one pixel apart from each other on all sides. As I worked, I noticed that the letters "T", "Y" and "I" were only six pixels across, and hugged the right edge of the block. When I finished, the result was a 133 x 33 GIF image with the CES's name painted on it; "THE CREAMY" on the first row, "ENTERTAINMENT" on the second row, and "SYSTEM" on the bottom. I'm very happy with the way the simulation turned out, and I plan on somehow using fine point markers to manually transfer that image onto my aluminum side panels, just above the green flames. 

5/21/2004 - I was sitting on the floor of my bedroom this morning, thinking about how to put CES's chassis together, when I peeked into my closet and saw something that could change the design of my front panel. In the plastic crate containing my various game controllers, I noticed one of my NES standard controllers and an idea just hit me from out of the blue. What if I could use the front plate of an NES controller to build a front panel faceplate for CES's USB and PS/2 ports? If I could do that, it would dramatically enhance the retro-gaming look that I want CES to have when I complete it. I was torn between preserving my controller and making CES look better. So I decided that I would only consider chopping down the controller if it met two criteria. First, the B and A button holes would have to be big enough to allow PS/2 ports to fit through them, and the Select and Start button holes would have to be big enough to accommodate USB ports. Second, the controller's faceplate had to fit into whatever room was still available on the front panel. (Any space that was not being used for the left-side buttons and LED shades, or the drives that will be in the upper right corner.) So I placed the controller on top of my front panel, and it fit between the LEDs/buttons and the drives. Then, I went next door to my sister's old bedroom, and got out an optical mouse that recently broke on me, and compared its USB connector to the Start button on the NES controller. The USB connector was bigger, but just barely, so I figured that if I decided to use the NES controller, I could bore the Start and Select buttons wider without severely altering the controller's look. I then compared the B and A buttons to the PS/2 end of a USB to PS/2 adapter, and found it to be a good fit.

> After putting a little more thought into using the controller on my front panel, I figured out how I'm going to prepare the controller for this use, and how to mount it within CES. First, I will take out the insides of the controller and detach the cord. I will then gradually bore the Select and Start buttons wider until USB ports can fit into them. After careful measuring and placement, I will cut holes in the back half of the controller, and then glue two USB ports and two PS/2 ports into place. If I do this right, when I put the front half of the controller over the back half, the ports should fit perfectly into the front half's holes. I'll then cut out a 4 3/4" x 2" rectangle in my front panel, just right of the LED covers, then glue the controller’s front half behind the hole. I will then use the back half's screw holes to attach it to the two expansion slot covers that my cold cathode fan grills' old switches were mounted on. Finally, I will use the slot covers’ screw notches to bolt them to CES’s bottom panel. This way, I can remove the front panel with the controller’s front half, while the PS/2 and USB ports remain on the back half.

5/19/2004 - The roller catches I bought yesterday at the Home Depot work in the same manner as a magnetic catch, however the rollers are a much better option for holding CES's top hatch doors closed for two reasons. First, the rollers don't use magnets, which can damage data on floppy disks. Second, rollers hold much tighter than magnets, so nothing short of a hearty tug will open CES's top hatch, which is exactly the result I want. In fact, if the roller catches hold tight enough, I may not need a sliding latch in addition to the rollers. The nuts and bolts and the catches I bought yesterday weigh about six and a half ounces, which is half an ounce less than the seven ounces I thought that 50 bolts and nuts and 1 sliding latch would weigh, so I ended up saving myself a little bit of weight.
