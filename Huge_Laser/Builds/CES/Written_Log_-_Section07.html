> Over the past two days, it's occurred to me that thumbscrews would probably not be the best option for keeping my top hatch closed. The hatch doors themselves will be 3/16" thick, and will rest on top of a 1/8" top angle iron, meaning for thumbscrews to be effective, they'd have to be half an inch long. I think it would be better to take advantage of my hatch method of internal access by considering the use of a latch that would be fixed to the inner edge of one of the hatch doors. In the latch's open position, it would lie parallel to the inner edge, and rest completely on the doors it's fastened to. I could close the latch by giving it a quarter turn, so that it covers the opposite door. I could buy one for each door, and put them on opposite ends of the inner edge, so that each door can hold the other closed. The next time I go to the Home Depot, I'll try to find the lightest latch possible, in order to keep the weight down on my computer.

> It's about a quarter-past 1PM, and I'm having some serious concerns about the top hatch of CES. If I decide to use a pair of latches to hold the hatch closed instead of thumbscrews, there's an issue of space I'll have to deal with. The brass handles are about 3 1/2" long, and will be mounted in the middle of the inner edge of the seven inch-deep hatch doors. Therefore, the only place left for me to mount my latches is the 1" x 3/4" space between the handles and where the top angle irons end. The latches will have to be less than two inches long, so they don't cover the top hatch windows in their closed position. I can't use sliding latches, because the only place of the doors where they won't pass over the windows is on the edges, and if I mount them there, the bolts holding the latches in place will run into the top angle irons, and may prevent the doors from fully closing. If I find that sliding bolts are my only option, I may have to drill holes in the top beams in order to accommodate the latches' bolts.

2/7/2004 - I went over to JD's house again today, this time to use his soldering iron to change the LED in my floppy drive, and to change the switches on my cold cathode grills. I was originally going to go tomorrow, but decided to go today so I could spend all day Sunday preparing for my Florida vacation when I visit my parents in Aventura. I had to use JD's soldering iron because my dad's soldering gun didn't work. As it turns out, this happened to be a blessing in disguise for two reasons. First, the kind of solder I was going to use was plumbing solder, which would have been way too thick to use on my drives. Second, the soldering gun was probably way too powerful for the transplant, and could have melted the floppy drive's circuit board, which would have obviously been no good.

> The first thing JD and I did was the LED transplant; taking the yellow LED out of my busted CD drive and replacing my floppy drive's green LED with it. So I opened up my CD drive again, and only took off the bottom cover. I was going to go further, but JD suggested that I didn't need to completely expose the LED, and that the solder could be melted from the opposite side of the circuit board. After the initial heating of the soldering iron, JD used the iron to melt the LED's solder, and the LED came out surprisingly easily. Working with the floppy drive proved to be a bit more of a hassle. I opened up the floppy drive, and only needed to take off the bottom cover. Like the CD drive, JD melted off the LED's solder from the back of the circuit board.

> The next step would have been to put the CD drive's old yellow LED into the floppy drive where its green one used to be, however I wanted to test to see not only if the yellow LED worked at all, but also to see if it truly was yellow. So JD got out his multimeter and ran a series of tests to see if the LED worked, without success. I thought the the LED was broken, and asked him to test the green one the he took out of the old drive. When he did, he got the same result. So he taped two AA batteries together with electrical tape, attached two wires of an antenna cord to the ends of the batteries, and the other end of the cord to the LED. When he tested the LED this time, it lit up yellow, and very brightly too. JD also tested the green LED just for the hell of it, and it too worked just fine.

> Once testing was was done, we were ready to put the yellow LED into the floppy drive. However, just when JD was about to put the LED in place, I asked him if he knew which way the LED was supposed to go. He didn't know yet, so he ran a test to find out. First, he needed to know which end of the LED was positive. He used the batteries to light the LED again, but this time he took note of which end was leading to the batteries' negative terminals. It turned out to be the the side which a grove within the LED was pointing towards, or the side of the internal metal plate that was thinner than the other side. Next, JD needed to find out which way the positive pin of the LED was to be mounted. I don't remember exactly how he did this, but it had something to do with the way the pins on the floppy drive's old LED were bent.

> Once the yellow LED's correct direction was determined, JD was ready to insert it where the floppy's old LED used to be. He bent the LED's pins to fit into two pin holes he drilled with a pindrill earlier. He snaked the LED through the arch in the floppy's frame, and pushed it into place from under the circuit board. Then, he soldered the new LED into place, being careful not to let to two solder points touch. JD was able to test if the LED still worked by putting the batteries to it again, and again it lit up. The LED stuck so far out beyond the arch, that we doubted that it would fit behind the drive's face plate. We considered taking the LED window off the plate, so that the LED could fit through it. However, when we put the faceplate on the drive, the LED surprisingly still fit behind it just fine!

> We moved on to task of changing the on/off switch on my cold cathode grills. JD used the multimeter to test if the switches worked, and both switches passed the test. He unscrewed the switch from one of the grill's faceplates, cut it's two wires with a wire cutter, stripped off the ends, then twisted them tightly. He then tinned the wires with the soldering iron and a bit of solder. In order to tin the contact points on switches, he needed to switch to the iron's higher setting, and use slightly thicker solder. When he cut off the excess length of wire, that completed the first switch. I took over for doing the second switch, with a little supervision of course. I did have a bit of a shaky start, but eventually I got the hang of soldering. This was a rather difficult soldering job, because the contacts were so thick, but I think I still did an OK job for a beginner.

> Unfortunately, I ran down the battery of the camera, and JD apparently didn't have a spare. There wasn't time to recharge the battery to take the soldering photos, so I don't have any pictures of the build. I obviously can't take pictures of anything irreversible we did, so I'm sorry to say that I've missed out on a lot of important shots. Oh well, that's just the way things go sometimes. :( On a positive note, JD did give me a pair of small, 2" long hinges I might be able to use for my top hatch. It's almost 11:30PM, and I'm eagerly waiting for myself to finish this part of the log so I can test out my modified equipment. I don't think I'm going to have any problems, but a lot of crazy things have happened with this project already, so I wouldn't rule out that possibility just yet.

> I just tested the floppy drive and one of the cold cathode grills. I hooked up the floppy drive to Polyfragger, but I never saw the light come on, even after I put a disk into it. Thinking I may have somehow broken the drive, I disconnected it and plugged it into Candybox. The drive worked without any problems, and the yellow light came on OK. Then, I re-assembled one of my grills and plugged it into one of Polyfragger's female Molex connectors. I turned on the computer, and when I flipped the grill's new switch, the grill glowed its bright green color. So everything basically came out OK today.

2/6/2004 - A few minutes before midnight, I began the task of chopping down my broken CD drive. My goal was to remove the LED without damaging it. Its pins were soldered onto the circuit board, and the drive's bottom cover was on top of the circuit board, sitting one or two millimeters away from the LED, so I couldn't just pull it out. Using the two screwdrivers in my PC repair toolkit, I took the CD drive apart piece by piece. I began by unscrewing the four screws at the bottom of the drive. This allowed me to take the bottom cover off of the drive, exposing not one, but two circuit boards. The large circuit board at the back had several screws on it. The other board was narrow and was roughly the shape of a stick of RAM, and was held down by only one screw. I took out all of the screws from the both boards, and only then did I realize that I only needed to unscrew to narrow board. Taking out the single screw on the narrow circuit board wasn't enough to dislodge it, and soon I found out why. There were two more screws, each on one of the upper corners, and I soon realized that these screws were inserted from the other side. This meant that taking off the narrow board and ultimately getting to the LED would mean getting to the other side of those screws. Eventually, I figured that the next step would be to take off the CD drive's top cover, which was held on by three shallow screws; one one the CD's left side, one on the right, and the other at the back. Once they were gone, and the top cover taken off, I could see the other side of the two screws I needed to undo. After I unscrewed those, I only needed to bend the sides of the drive outward a little bit to wiggle the narrow circuit board out of place. Now, the only thing holding the board in place was a thin ribbon cable. While I was dissecting the CD drive, three of the tabs I had broken while taking off its faceplate fell out of the drive.

> The yellow LED had two prongs, which went through two tiny holes in the circuit board, and held on by a bit of solder. The prongs were bent forward at a 90 degree angle, so I bent them straight upwards to get a better idea of how to take them out. I remembered that on the web page on www.ochw.com I was reading only a few hours ago, the man who was changing the LED of his floppy drive had taken out his old LED by unsoldering it. He said he put the soldering iron to the old LED, and heated it just long enough to melt the solder, so he could remove the LED without damaging the drive. I went back upstairs to Candybox to take another look at the page, just to make sure I wasn't missing anything too important. I then headed to the garage to look for my dad's soldering gun. I checked a blue metal case, and while I didn't find the soldering gun, I did find Dad's Dremmel, something that he'd been unable to find for months. I learned how to use such a tool last weekend while I was cutting out one of CES's top hatch windows last weekend at JD's house, so I can use this Dremmel for various parts of my project when I'm not visiting JD. I also found two constant contact switches which will be great replacements for the ones on my cold cathode grills. I found the solder, but after searching the garage fairly thoroughly, I couldn't find the soldering gun.

> It's seven PM, and I'm about to unsolder the CD drive's yellow LED. I've got my workstation set up in the kitchen, and I've found the soldering gun, because when I called my dad in Aventura, he told me where to find it. Unfortunately, JD and Jen's Nikon digital camera ran out of batteries while I was taking pictures today. So unfortunately, there will be a photo log blackout for this soldering job. Because I will be doing a lot of irreversible work today, I will be missing out on a lot of potential pictures. Oh well, maybe I can re-enact this transplant someday, just for the photos.

> It turns out that the camera has power after all. Unfortunately, it won't last too long, so I'll have to do everything I can to conserve power. There are three ways I will deal with this problem. First, I will take shots only when I'm about to do something irreversible, and take everything else when I have the power. Second, I will only take one picture per item. I'll need to anyway, since I only have about six shots left. Third, I will try to leave the camera on at twenty second intervals, and no longer. The camera's reliability will certainly help here.

> I plugged in the soldering gun and tried to melt off the solder that was holding the CD drive's LED into place. However, the solder showed no signs of melting. I thought the tip of the gun might have been rusted, so I grabbed a cylindrical file from the garage, and filed some of the rust off. After two hours of unsuccessfully trying to melt the LED's solder in my attempt to dislodge it, I finally gave up. I then tried melting some of the solder just to see what would happen, but it didn't even get warm. So I think it's a safe bet that the gun is busted, and that I won't be using it again. It looks like I'll be doing this at JD's place this weekend when I try it with his soldering iron. I didn't need the camera, since I had no progress to photograph.

> With nothing better to do, I took apart my floppy drive, just to make the process easier when I finally do it at JD's place this weekend. My goal was to access the LED and examine it. The face plate was already off, so that was one step already out of the way. I only needed to remove two screws to take off the bottom cover to get a better view of the LED, which turned out to be 3mm, and fastened to the underside of the circuit board. In order to see what was under the LED, I removed the only screw on the circuit board, which loosened it. Wiggling the board from under two hooks in the front that were holding it in place loosened the board further. The tip of the LED stuck through some kind of arc-shaped hole in the frame, holding that part of the board down, so I had to bend the LED down in order to get it free. There were three more hooks at the back, which were holding that part of the circuit board in place, but I decided to leave those alone in fear of snapping one of the four thin copper wires that attached the board to the rest of the drive.

Unsuccessful, I tried searching for one with a blue LED. Since my broken floppy drive is no good for this, I think that I might be able to practice on a broken CD drive. > The other thing I wanted to change is the on/off switch on my cold cathode grills. The tiny red switch on faceplates may be a bit too small for a finger to reach them behind the front panel. If this is the case, I have at least two small switches in the garage amongst the screws that may be better suited for the panel.
