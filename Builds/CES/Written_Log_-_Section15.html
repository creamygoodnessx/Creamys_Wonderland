> I stopped by JD's house yesterday, and brought my Playstation 2 so I could show them the Simpsons: Hit & Run game. I also brought along CES and all of its parts. I explained to JD my plan to mount reset button and drives to CES. When I mentioned how I planned on mounting the reset button, he suggested I use a special drill bit called a unibit. It wasn't until later in the night that I understood that a unibit is a cone shaped drill bit, just like the one that Peter used to make the drill holes for CES. JD didn't have such a bit handy, but he suggested that he could pick one up at the local hardware store the next day after work, and then pick me up so we could work on CES. It's just after 1PM, and JD said that he gets off from work at around 4PM, so I still have some time to make a few final preparations.

> At around 3:30PM, JD stopped by at my house and picked me up. I took along CES and all of its parts, but this time around I left the Playstation 2 at home. We stopped by a hardware store in town and bought a cone-shaped drill bit, also known as a unibit. The unibit cost nearly forty bucks, so JD and I had to split the cost 50/50. After we arrived at JD's house, he began preparing a spaghetti and meatball dinner, while I took a rest on the couch and played with my nephew Austin. After watching a couple episodes of Monster Garage, we went to the basement to begin work on CES. JD examined the chassis, and then I removed the top-front 12" angle iron to make the job easier. He then used a standard drill bit to make a hole directly in the center of the reset button mark on the beam. He then opened the unibit's packaging, and I touched the bit for a second and noticed that it was covered in grease. JD switched to the unibit, and drilled a few notches down, and then checked the size of the hole by putting the reset button into it. The hole was too small, so he drilled another notch down on the unibit. He kept repeating the pattern until the hole was just the right size. As it turns out, I had marked the hole precisely in the right spot; any higher, and the unibit would have run into the horizontal side of the angle iron, any lower, and the hole would have gone over the vertical side's edge, resulting in an incomplete circle for a hole.

> JD and I took a break to have dinner, and then returned to the basement to finish the job. JD mentioned that he could use something called a threading kit to fit the reset button's bolt hole firmly into place. A threading tool can allow a bolt to screw into place tightly like a wood screw. He said that the worst thing that could happen was that he could drill the hole too big, but he could then pop out the threading, and then fit the bolt in place with a nut the other end. I agreed to go the threading route, and soon JD was searching for his threading kit. It took him a while to find it, but once he did, he proceeded to make a test hole on a scrap yardstick-like piece of aluminum. I thought that one of the angle-headed 8/32" bolts that I used to fit the hinges onto my top hatch doors would suffice, but the fit in the reset button's bolt hole was terrible. JD found a steel bolt that was slightly narrower than one of my 8/32" bolts, and the head was the right shape to fit into the hole. JD drilled a hole in the test aluminum piece with one of the smaller drill bits, but the hole was too small, so he used the next biggest bit. He threaded the hole, but the hole was too big for the bolt, causing the bolt to wobble. We then tried one my own flat-bottom bolts, and it was a much better fit.

> With the size of the reset button's bolt hole established, and with the fitting bolt picked out, JD proceeded to work on the real hole on the 12" angle iron. He carefully rotated the reset button, so that its bolt hole lined up with the horizontal mark. The button was somewhat unstable, so he had to hold it firmly in position while drilling the hole. He drilled partially into the beam, and the hole appeared to be perfectly centered on the line. JD then removed the button to prevent it from being damaged, drilled through the hole the rest of the way, and then used the threading tool to thread the hole. I tried out the holes in the angle iron to confirm that the reset button and its bolt could fit them. I fed the button wire end first into the hole, and the button's body fit snugly into place. Then, I lined up the button's bolt hole with the corresponding hole in the angle iron, and started to bolt it in place. JD warned me that over-tightening the bolt could damage the threading, so I screwed the bolt on just tight enough to get a snug fit. Although it probably wasn't necessary, I fitted a nut onto the other side of the bolt just to make sure the button stayed on. Satisfied with the fit and done for the day, I bolted the angle iron back to the front of the chassis, and put everything away. JD was kind enough to let me borrow his hobby saw, and once I was packed up, I got a ride home from my dad who came to pick me up.

11/16/2004 - Now that the hole for the reset button's position is marked, I plan to use JD's drill press this weekend so I can drill the hole. I will then feed the button through the hole, plug end first, line up the button's bolt hole with the horizontal center line, drill the hole, and then fix the button in place with a bolt. With the button's position firmly established, I will know the exact position of the front panel, which I will put into position, and fix on temporarily with duct tape. From there, I can install the other front panel components; the slimline drive mounting rack, the PS/2 USB panel, the power button, and the power and HDD LEDs.

> I remeasured the big hole in the cardboard simulation of the reset button panel, and found it to be 19 millimeters wide, rather that the 17 mm I thought it was. This means that my reset button hole is marked 2 mm too small. However, I'm going to leave the hole mark in the chassis the way it is. This way, I can undersize the hole when I drill it, and then make it bigger gradually until it's exactly the right size.

> Earlier this afternoon, I wanted to find out exactly where the slimline mounting rack will end up within CES. Because I knew the reset button's exact position, I in turn knew the front panel's exact position. I laid CES on its back end, carefully put the panel in place, and temporarily attached it to the front of the chassis with masking tape. Then, I used my pink mechanical pencil to make a mark through the hole I cut out for the slimline CD drive. But then, I couldn't figure out what to do next. The old CD drive hole told me where I could place the mounting rack, but I still had no idea how to put the rack into position to mark the holes on the bottom panel. So I decided to take the panel off until I could get a better idea of what the hell I was doing.

> However, before I removed the panel, I put my homemade medicine bottle power button into the panel's green bottle top, in order to get an idea of how to attach it permanently to the chassis. Somehow, the button stuck to the back of the bottle top on its own. After studying the back of the button for about a minute, I figured out how to put it permanently into place. The back of the button (the bottom of the medicine bottle) was almost in line with the front 12" angle irons' inner-most edges. I believe that I can fix the button in place by bolting the horizontal end of a thin angle iron to the bottom of the top-front 12" beam. The vertical end would point downward, and extend about an inch and a half. On the inner edge of the vertical end of the thin angle iron, I would attach the back of the power button. There is a big copper spring inside the button, allowing me to put a tiny screw through the back of the button and inside the spring. This is the only way I can get a screw in there without it getting in the way of the spring. After I was satisfied with where I was planning to put the power button, I took off the front panel and erased the drive rack mark on the chassis.

> As I typed out the above paragraph, I came up with another method of determining the correct position of the slimline mounting rack. Since the rack's position inside the chassis is not as important as that of the reset button, I felt that I could cut the hole for the rack in my front panel before bolting the rack to the bottom panel. I knew how far left and right the rack would be (Along the right edge of the front panel), and how far into the chassis it would go (rack's faceplate the same depth as the front panel), but I hadn't yet determined how high off the bottom panel it would be. If I put it too high, it would run into the top-front 12" angle iron. Too low, and it would clash with the hard drive. My plan was to cut the existing slimline CD drive hole bigger to fit the rack's faceplate. The first thing I did was determine the height of the bottom of the front panel compared to the bottom panel. I put the front panel into position and held it there with my hand. I could see a little piece of the bottom panel from under the front-left 4" angle iron, and so I was able to compare its height to the bottom of the front panel. They appeared to be very close in height, but the front panel looked to be very slightly higher.

> The next step was to take a few measurements to find out how much space there was between the hard drive and the horizontal edge of the top-front 12" angle iron. I decided that the best way to do this was to first put a mounting rack faceplate on the front panel where I felt the rack would go. Instead of using the beige faceplate already on the mounting rack, I felt it was easier to use the detached black faceplate instead. I placed the plate face down against the front panel's right edge, and made sure the top of the plate ran along the CD bay hole. That position looked good, so I decided to go with it and get on with the measurements. I used my aluminum ruler to measure the height of the faceplate, which turned out to be about 42 millimeters. I then retrieved the empty brass hard drive cooling mount and my broken Maxtor hard drive from my bedroom, put the two together the same way I plan to put together the real drive and cooler, and measured their combined height, which was just over 40 millimeters. Then, with the faceplate still in position, I measured the distance between the bottom of the panel and the bottom of the faceplate, which was about 45 millimeters. Since the height of the drive plus the cooler was only 40mm, there will be only 5mm separating the bottom of the slimline mounting rack and the top of the hard drive. In order to get a visual sense of what this will look like, I put the empty drive cooler on top of the broken hard drive, stood the front panel up against the drive and cooler, and manually held the slimline drive rack faceplate in its place on the panel. Despite the closeness of the components, I think it will all fit just fine.

> Tomorrow, I plan to cut the rectangular hole for the mounting rack in the front panel. I will undersize it so I can make adjustments if necessary. If it turns out that the hole will be too low, I can cut the space above it and vice versa. This is the first irreversible step that I've done to the panel in a long time so I'm very nervous about doing it. Once the hole has been cut, I will then determine exactly how to mount the rack inside the chassis, as well as the hard drive itself.

11/15/2004 - When I recently studied how the front panel would fit onto CES's chassis, I noticed that the reset button would probably be slightly too high to fit behind the hole in the panel. I soon realized that the margin of error of the placement of the reset button is much slimmer than any other component on the front panel. The button will be fitted to the top-front 12" angle iron, but since the beam is only 1" wide, there's not a lot of places the button can go. If the button is too high, I will be forced to cut the horizontal side of the beam to make room for it. It the button is too low, part of the hole for the button will pass below the vertical side of the beam. On the other hand, the other front panel components, such as the power button and slimline drive rack, have a lot of places they can go, and their positions are less dependent to whatever they're anchored to. It's for this reason that I've decided to fit the reset button into place before the other front panel components.

> I already know the horizontal position of the front panel; right smack in the middle of the front of the chassis. I also know the reset button's vertical position; As high on the top 12" beam as it will go without clashing with the beam's horizontal side. Determining the exact positions of both the button and panel will just be a matter of putting the panel exactly in the middle of the front of the chassis, marking the button's horizontal position through the panel's reset button hole, removing the panel, moving the button to its vertical position, and then marking that position. Several months ago, I made a cardboard simulation to help myself determine the positions of the power and reset buttons, and the power and HDD LEDs, so I could make a single mount for all of them. The simulation had two holes for the reset button; a big hole for the button itself to fit through, and a nearby smaller hole for the fastening bolt. Two days ago, I put the front panel where I felt it should go on the chassis, and used my pink mechanical pencil to make a rectangular mark through the panel's reset button hole. Using that reset button mark as a guide, I will copy the hole pattern to the 12" angle iron. When I get a chance, I will use either Peter's or JD's drill press to drill the two holes, and then fit the button into position.

> This morning, I got to work on marking the position of where the reset button is going to go the chassis. I did all of today's work with the chassis resting on its back end, so that the front end faced upwards. My first concern was to figure out the horizontal position of the button. This wasn't too hard to figure out; all I had to do was place the front panel in the middle of the front of the chassis. The rectangular mark on the chassis lined up with the reset button hole, so I removed the panel, and used my pink pencil to draw a vertical line through the middle of the mark. With the horizontal position marked, my next concern was to figure out the reset button's vertical position, but this was a lot trickier. When I drill the reset button hole in the vertical side of the chassis' top-front 12" angle iron, the hole has to be clear of the angle iron's horizontal side. So I retrieved my aluminum ruler from my bedroom, and measured the thickness of one of CES's angle irons, which turned out to be about three millimeters. So I drew several dots along the top-front 12" beam, each three millimeters from the top edge, and then used the ruler's edge to bridge the dots while I connected the dots with a single line. This line was to mark how far up the reset button could go without running into the horizontal edge of the beam.

> Next, I placed the reset button face down on the vertical edge of the 12" beam, with one of its edges running along the horizontal line, with the button's bolt hole on the right side. I used my pink pencil to mark the outline of the hole. I then used the same method to mark a few more bolt hole marks across the angle iron. I laid the ruler on top of the 4" beams on either side of the 12" beam, and began to draw a horizontal line across the circular marks. But before I could finish the line, I realized that the marks were uneven, so I stopped and erased the partially drawn line and the marks. I then tried a different method of determining the vertical position of the reset button. I measured the distance between the middle of the reset button and its right edge, which turned out to be 10 millimeters. So I made a few marks on the 12" beam, each exactly 10 millimeters from the horizontal line that marked the inner edge of the beam's horizontal side, and then used my pink pencil to connect the dots.

> Once the horizontal line was drawn, that line, along with the vertical line I drew earlier, formed a cross, and told me where the best position to put the reset button was. This position was a little lower than the center of the old rectangular reset button mark, which meant that the front panel would have to sit lower than I originally planned it to. To remove any future confusion, I erased the rectangle, leaving only the cross. The next step was to place the cardboard button hole simulation over the plus to draw the button's drill hole. In order to make this task a little easier, I marked five and ten millimeter marks from the center of the plus, making the plus look like crosshairs. But the reset button holes in the cardboard simulation were too far away from the sides of the sheet to put directly over the hole, because the left 4" angle iron was in the way. So I put the left side of the simulation on top of the 4" beam, and looked for something to rest the right side of the sheet on. I eventually came across a part of an old speaker, rested that on top of the chassis, and rested the cardboard sheet on top of the speaker part and the left 4" beam. I put the sheet's big hole over the plus sign on the chassis, with the small hole to the right of the big one, and directly over the horizontal line. The speaker piece kept sliding as I was getting ready to make the marks, so I taped it to the chassis, afterwhich it held solidly in place. After I marked the two holes in the chassis, I removed the speaker piece from the chassis, but when I did that, the small hole mark came off with the tape that was holding the speaker piece in place.

> I put the front panel back into place, but this time I lowered it slightly to accommodate the reset button's new position. The bottom of the panel was no longer running along the bottom edges of the 4" beams, but was now slightly below them. The panel's horizontal position was exactly in the middle of the chassis, and the plus sign on the chassis was exactly centered behind the reset button hole in the panel. However, when I removed the panel, I noticed that the big circle was not centered around the plus sign. So I erased the circle, and decided to try a new method of drawing it. I measured the width of the big hole in the simulation, which turned out to be about 17 millimeters across. Half of 17 is eight and a half, so using my black Pigma Micron pen, I drew four dots on the plus sign, each exactly eight and a half millimeters from the center. I also used the Micron to thicken the center of the plus. I decided that drawing another circle was not necessary, and neither was replacing the small hole mark that came off with the speaker piece. The Micron marks could tell the drill bit where to go just fine, so I left it like that.

11/14/2004 - For the past few days, I was planning on making a pair of keyhole-shaped holes in CES's front 4" angle irons, and attach corresponding pegs in the front panel so I can slide the panel downwards into place. The panel could then be fastened on by a single thumbscrew. Today, I realized that this option of attaching the panel would not be possible. This is because the face of the slimline mounting rack would stick through its slot in the panel, and would not let the panel slide into place. The only way to get the front panel on the chassis is to push it straight forward, and somehow fasten it into place. As of right now, my plan of attaching the panel is to simply fit it into place, and lock it in with a single thumbscrew in the bottom-middle of the panel that will go through the panel's rectangular notch. If this notch ends up completely above the bottom 12" angle iron, I can attach a small piece of aluminum to the angle iron behind the notch, so I can fit on a thumbscrew. By the time CES is completely finished, it will have not only the drive rack sticking through it, but also the faceplate for the PS/2 and USB ports. These two things should keep the panel from shifting and turning, but it still may not be enough to keep the panel still, even with the thumbscrew. If it's not, I may have to add more thumbscrews.

> I was studying the front panel again just now, and I noticed two tiny notches on the bottom-left and bottom-right corners of the frame. If I do decide to go with more thumbscrews, I may place one screw through each of these two notches. The 4" beams will be just behind the notches, so I won't have to make any special attachments to the chassis to put thumbscrews into those notches.

11/13/2004 - Three days ago, I was working on transplanting hardware components from Ray_Carmen, my parents' old computer, and into Polyfragger, the computer I got from my A+ Certification course at Fairleigh Dickinson University. Ray_Carmen's motherboard was fried, and Polyfragger lacked hard drives, so I felt that the logical thing to do was to merge the two machines together. Now, Ray_Carmen's drives and expansion cards are combined with Polyfragger's chassis, motherboard, and power supply. Since this new machine is the cross between Polyfragger and Ray_Carmen, I am now calling it PolyCarmen.

> Anyways, as I was finishing the merger, and closing up Ray_Carmen's old and empty chassis, I noticed that I didn't need a screwdriver or conventional screws to close one of the side panels. Instead, there were three slits on the side of the chassis on the top, and three on the bottom. The panel had corresponding tabs that fit into the holes, and I could close it by placing it against the chassis, and then sliding it to one side. The panel also had a thumbscrew permanently attached to it, and this locked the panel into place. I began to think that I could implement something similar to CES's front panel. I'm typing this out on Glamdring in the kitchen, and I have CES's chassis in front of me. I've been spending the past few hours studying the chassis and the plastic front panel, trying to figure out how to attach the two together. I noticed a small rectangular indentation in the front of the panel, centered and towards the bottom. I figured this notch would be a good place to put a thumbscrew, should I decide to go with that option. However, a single thumbscrew will not be enough to hold the front panel securely in place. Right now, the only thing I can think of is making keyhole-shaped holes in the 4" angle irons, and corresponding pegs in the panel. But there may be many options I haven't thought of yet, so hopefully something will pop into me soon.

> I've noticed a few other things while studdying the front panel. With CES's chassis resting on its back end, I placed the panel so that it was horizontally centered on the 4" beams, and that the bottom of the panel rested along the bottom of the beams. The left 4" beam is slightly higher than the one on the right, but the front panel is crooked in the same way, so the two imperfections cancel each other out. While the front panel was in place, I noticed that the hole for the reset button was very high on the top 12" beam, and that this could force the top side of the button over the top of the 12" beam. When I get the chance, I'll measure the button to see how high it can go, and if the reset button hole on the front panel lines up with it. But for now, I'm going to hit the sack, since it's after 7AM and I've been up all night.

11/1/2004 - Just after 11PM last night, I began to reassemble CES's chassis, a task that extended into the next day. I retrieved the top and bottom assemblies, and the back panel of CES from my bedroom, and laid them out on the kitchen table. I then bolted together the left and right assemblies. I knew that putting the panels on the right way may be tricky, because Peter and I had drilled the holes for the left and right panels to be reversed. Some of the holes may not line up to let the bolts through, and there was the possibility of me having to drill new holes.

> I began by attaching the right assembly to the right side of the bottom assembly. The bottom-left bolt attached the two assemblies together OK, but it was when I tried to put the bottom-right bolt in place that I started having problems. I fit a bolt through the panels successfully and thought everything was OK, but I soon realized the right assembly was crooked and had to be fixed. So I went to the basement pantry and retrieved my dad's Panasonic hand drill, and a box of 20 drill bits. I changed to the 11/64" drill bit, and held the right assembly so it was as parallel to the table as I could get it. I put the drill bit through the hole at the bottom of the Fire Flower angle iron and tried to drill through the bottom 7" right-side beam, but I couldn't keep the chassis still. So I called my mother to hold the chassis while I drilled the hole. I was able to successfully make the hole, and the right assembly was fairly even. But it was late at night and my mother had to go to bed, so I had to look for other ways to hold down the chassis while I drilled.

> Next up was attaching the left assembly to its place on the bottom assembly, but this turned out to be even more of a bitch than the right side. After bolting the bottom-left corner of the left assembly to the bottom assembly, I lifted up the right side of the left assembly so it was as close to parallel to the table as I could get it, and used Scotch tape to hold it in place. I then grabbed a deck of blue Bicycle playing cards from a nearby drawer, made a stack at the height of the left side of the left assembly, and slid the stack under the right side. I taped the stack together so the cards wouldn't fly apart. There was already a drill hole on the front side of the Koopa Troopa angle iron, but the matching hole in the bottom-front 12" beam didn't match it exactly, so I fixed that with the drill. I then put a bolt though the hole, and secured it with a nut. This made the chassis more stable a I drilled the hole in the 7" left-side angle iron through the painted-side bottom hole in the Fire Flower beam. I then put in a bolt and secured it with a nut, and the side panels were finally secured to the proper positions. Luckily, I managed to keep the left assembly fairly parallel to the table.

> The next step was to fit on the top assembly, and when it bolted on without me having to drill any new holes, I was overjoyed, and I thought I was nearing the end of the re-assembly of CES. However, I still had to put on the back panel. There were bolt holes already on the top of the back side of the back 4" angle irons, but I couldn't use them because any bolt going though those holes would run into other bolts in the area. So I drilled a new hole in the top-left corner of the back of CES just to the right of the old one, but as soon as I pulled the drill out of the hole, the angle irons shifted, putting the holes out of alignment. I soon realized that opening the hatch doors all the way could cause the top 12" beams to shift positions, and that I could only put a bolt through the hole when the door was closed or ajar.

> Once I fitted a bolt into the new hole and firmly held together the top-left corner of the chassis, I was about to drill a hole in the top-right corner of the back of CES, when I began to wonder if the back panel could fit between the back of the bottom assembly, and the inner side of the back 4" beams. There was a gap between the left assembly and the bottom assembly to accommodate the panel, but not on the right side. As a result, I couldn't get the back panel on. So I did a test to find out what adjustment I would have to make to get the panel to fit. I removed the two left-side bolts that would hold the back panel in place, and the bottom-right bolt on the right assembly. I then slid the back panel in place, and taped it there. The panel wasn't even, but it didn't have to be for the time being; I just wanted to see how the back-right 4" angle iron would fit around the back panel. Not surprisingly, I wasn't able to put the bottom-right right assembly bolt into place, but looking at the bolt from the inside of the chassis told me that the bolt hole had to be extended to the right (from the outside), towards the back of the chassis. So I took CES apart and isolated the bottom assembly, so I wouldn't risk damaging the side assemblies. I then drilled into the hole at an angle, towards the back of the assembly. Not sure if I pushed the hole far enough to the right, I put the chassis back together and tried again to fit the back panel into place. The bolt fit though the hole, but it was leaning towards the left.
